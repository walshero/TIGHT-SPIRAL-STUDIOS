That is what a humanist who builds systems actually does: not invent from nothing, but compound what's already understood until it becomes an instrument.

Now ride your instrument through six stations. Each one is a move in how I think — and each carries a small working thing you can actually touch.

Katamari: the ball that grows by rolling — accrete what fits, then graduate it into something whole. A studio runs on the same physics.
Station 1

Notice — the problem a real person has.

A conference room, my college, assessment review day. Someone proposes adding a second year of reflection before we act on what we found. Reasonable. Everyone nods. Then the arithmetic lands on me: a second year means our five learning outcomes get assessed on a ten-year cycle. Ten years to find out whether a change helped a student. I do the math twice because I don't want it to be right.

—

Every system I build starts here: not with a tool, but with a person stuck inside machinery nobody owns because we've all agreed to rent it. The humanist move is to start from the stuck person, not the available technology.

The constraint was never the problem. The constraint was the design. Build for the person who struggles most to use the thing, and it gets better for everyone.
Station 2

Frame — make many readings converge.

—

This is Confluence, the assessment architecture underneath the studio. Three readers look at the same student work and score it. Alone, three opinions are noise. The system's job is to make them converge on a defensible shared judgment — and to show where they don't yet agree, so the disagreement becomes the work.

Touch the move — raters converge
Three readers scored the same piece. Tap to reveal each. Watch the band narrow toward agreement.
Agreement: waiting for all three readers…
The full build: Confluence calibrates raters against a validated rubric (MassBay placement, 1–6) before any real student work is scored.
Station 3

Build — let the noise subtract to what counts.

—

Here's a game I shipped for my own creative-writing students in their last week. The curriculum sheet lists everything equally, forever. The game inverts it: it starts as everything-shouting, and the student's real answers subtract until only what counts is lit. You learn what matters by watching the list get shorter and truer.

Touch the move — the wall subtracts
It's the last week. Tap the two things that actually decide the grade now. The rest is noise — watch it fade.
Tap what still counts.
The full build: "What Counts Now" — a token economy where finished workshops build the portfolio, with a comfort gate and a copy-out the student chooses to share.
Station 4

Read the gap — between what was said and what was true.

—

This one teaches a single habit: notice the distance between the words you were given and the record you weren't. You read a real thing a leader said, decide how much you trust it — then the game shows you what the original audience never got to see, and lets you feel how far the words moved you first.

Touch the move — trust, then see the record
A leader, 1964: "renewed hostile actions against United States ships on the high seas." How much do you trust it, on the words alone?
The record turns: later analysis indicates the second attack that justified escalation most likely never happened — the ships were running covert operations the public wasn't told about. The gap between the sentence and the record is the whole lesson. Noticing it is the win.
The full build: Choose Your Leader — dated, sourced records; a trust-delta that you feel before you judge; trauma-informed rails so consequence lands on the rhetoric's reach, never your worth.
Station 5

See your own frame — the move you can't make from inside.

—

The hardest one. Once, deep in designing a game about how framing controls what we see, the studio had built forty-one cross-referenced documents about the game instead of the game. We had made a Rube Goldberg machine out of the meeting about the Rube Goldberg machine. The caucus had become the bit. That's the thesis of the whole laugh-factory game: you cannot see your own frame from inside it — you need a move, or a person, that stands outside.

Touch the move — step outside the frame
Read the sentence. Then tap to step outside it.
"We just need one more planning document before we start building."
From inside: responsible. From outside: the planning is the avoidance. The frame felt like diligence; named from outside, it's the thing keeping you from the build. The laugh is the recognition — and the recognition is the exit.
The full build: the terministic-screen game — players feel a frame govern their reading, then get the outside view that the frame was a choice.
Station 6 · the spur

The method leaves the building.

—

Everything so far lives inside a classroom. But the moves don't know they're in a classroom. Notice the stuck person. Make readings converge. Subtract the noise. Read the gap. See the frame. Point that at a city's transit system instead of a course, and the shape holds: the rider stuck on a delayed line is the stuck person; the competing agency priorities are the readings that must converge; the budget noise subtracts to what actually moves people; the gap between the published schedule and the lived commute is the record turning.

Your instrument — your compound — is the right tool for it, because a transit network is the thing your metaphor describes: streams converging, a line of stations, current that loops and returns.

Honest flag — what this is and isn't

This station shows the method reaching toward a real system. It is not a transit model. A physics-accurate MBTA simulation is a different, much larger build — real network data, real infrastructure constraints, a real engineering pipeline — and it is named as the next project, not this one. The humanist move is to know the difference between a metaphor that travels and a model that has to be earned with data.

The argument you just lived

Why publish a paper this way.

A traditional paper is built for one imagined reader: the gatekeeper of its discipline. Everyone else — the administrator, the skeptical colleague, the student, the person who reads slowly, the person who can't easily see the screen — has to fight the form to get the value out. The gate isn't a quality filter. It's a design that excludes by default.

This paper did the opposite. You chose your comfort. You chose your metaphor, and then a second, and the paper rendered in your compound. You didn't read about adaptive thinking — you performed it, and the artifact adapted to you. The argument and the experience were the same object. That's the publishing method: the reader's interest, expertise, semiotic domain, and use reshape the artifact, instead of the reader bending to a form built for someone else.

First test population: every academic discipline at my college. If a creative-writing teacher and a biotech student can both ride the same argument through their own compound, the method scales. That's the next thing to prove.

The Garden of Forking Paths: hold every branch honestly. The metaphors you didn't pick — and the city model not yet built — wait in the parking lot, not the void.
