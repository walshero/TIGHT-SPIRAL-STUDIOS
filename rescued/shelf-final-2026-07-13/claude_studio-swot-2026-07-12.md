# OUTSIDER'S SWOT — TIGHT SPIRAL STUDIOS
*Take-stock after the 2026-07-12 reorg. Written from the outside, on purpose.*
*The vantage: a skeptical ed-tech operator who has watched a lot of one-person studios build a beautiful factory and never ship the car. Not a booster. Matt asked for the outside read; this is it.*

---

## WHAT AN OUTSIDER SEES IN ONE LINE

A one-person studio with a genuinely uncommon asset — pedagogy and accessibility treated as *engineering*, not decoration — that is one good quarter of shipping away from mattering, and one more governance layer away from disappearing into its own machinery.

Today's reorg (Horvath as PM, the new room, the Tableau Gate with real teeth, moves-not-verdicts, a standing sweep) is the right *kind* of change: it converts taste into checks and hands execution to the belt. The risk is not the direction. The risk is that the studio keeps sharpening the saw and the tree stays standing.

---

## STRENGTHS

- **Accessibility as arithmetic is a real moat.** Almost nobody builds "contrast is a computation, exit 1 if it fails" into their pipeline. For a designer with retinitis pigmentosa building for real classrooms, this is both authentic and, in higher ed, a compliance-grade differentiator. The Tableau Gate extends it from color to *first-second legibility*. That is defensible and rare.
- **Checks over wishes.** The studio's own hard rule — "if a rule can't be a check, it's a wish" — is exactly the discipline most creative shops lack. Today it was honored: the scene-first floor stopped being prose and became `one-thing-gate.py`. That is a maturity signal.
- **Pedagogy is load-bearing, not sprinkled on.** Horvath, Gee, Squire, Le Guin aren't marketing. The mechanics actually encode attention-gating, regime-of-competence, carrier-bag structure. The games teach *because of* the design, not despite it.
- **Portability by design.** Single-file, offline, no external hosts, portable Markdown canon. Survives tool changes, vendor churn, and subscription death. This is the right architecture for a resource-constrained, one-person operation.
- **A working design vocabulary.** "The belt," "the room," "lanes," "moves not verdicts." Shared language is how a solo studio scales its own thinking. It exists and it's used.

## WEAKNESSES

- **Governance is outrunning output.** 173 project docs, ~30 rules, four lanes, three guards, a charter, a constitution — and the visible product is a handful of teaching games. To an outsider this is the tell: the studio spends more energy on *how it works* than on *what it ships*. The scaffolding is impressive; the shipped shelf is thin by comparison.
- **Bus factor of one.** Every seat in "the room" is a simulated voice; every real decision is Matt. Delegation charters describe authority, but the actual founder gate is on nearly everything that matters. If Matt is out for a month, the studio stops.
- **Canon sprawl is itself a risk the studio already knows about.** The project instructions are dense with anti-drift rules (v33 vs v34, triple-`Claude_files`, "canon is computed not remembered"). That volume of guardrail is evidence the drift problem is real and recurring, not solved. More lanes create more places for truth to diverge.
- **The reorg is churn.** Changing the PM, the room, and the pipeline in one day is destabilizing even when each change is right. Nothing has been *run through* the new order yet except one bite-test. Until the belt clears real games under the new gate, this is a plan, not a result.
- **No external validation loop.** Everything is judged by internal panels and Matt's eye. No students, no faculty peers, no play data from real classrooms is in the loop yet. The studio is grading its own homework.

## OPPORTUNITIES *(Eagle Eye)*

- **Package the accessibility gate as a product, not just a practice.** "A pre-ship legibility + contrast gate for educational web content" is something colleges, disability-services offices, and OER projects need and cannot buy off the shelf. This is a workshop, a consulting line, a grant deliverable, and a conference talk — from tooling Matt already built. Highest ROI, lowest new build.
- **The games are OER with a story.** Cliché Field/Line/City, Funny Boney's, the Confluence assessment tools — released under open license with attribution, they're portfolio, recruiting-for-the-lit-mag, and a credible basis for an accessibility-plus-AI-in-higher-ed grant. The pedagogy documentation already exists; that's usually the hard part.
- **"Studio OS" as a template for other faculty.** The lane/belt/guard system, stripped of TSP specifics, is a personal-operating-system other overloaded educators would adopt. That's a talk and possibly a small paid template — and it feeds the AI Task Force mandate directly.
- **AI Task Force leverage.** Everything here — governance, accessibility, checks-not-wishes, human-in-the-loop — is exactly what an institutional AI policy needs as a worked example. The studio is a live case study Matt can bring to committee instead of abstractions.
- **The standing sweep is a demo.** "An agent that audits my whole shelf weekly and hands me improved builds, not a to-do list" is a compelling, concrete story for skeptical faculty about what agentic AI actually does.

## THREATS

- **Sustainability / burnout.** One person, a dozen committees, a magazine, teaching, and a studio this elaborate. The most likely failure mode is not a bad decision — it's the founder running out of runway and the machine going quiet. The governance load is a contributor, not just a victim, here.
- **Tool-chasing and optimization-for-its-own-sake** — Matt's own named failure modes — are structurally encouraged by a studio that rewards clever new gears. The reorg itself could be an instance. Watch for it.
- **FERPA and public-web exposure.** The rules are strong, but the surface area (four lanes, public Netlify/Pages, scheduled agents) is large, and the consequence of one miss is severe and institutional, not cosmetic. This stays a real threat as long as any lane touches public web.
- **Institutional politics and faculty skepticism.** An English professor shipping AI-built games and an "AI studio" invites both turf and credibility challenges. Without external validation (above), critics get to define what this is.
- **Persona-dependency.** The room is a strength, but leaning on simulated Horvath/Gee/Le Guin can substitute confident narration for real user testing. The map is not the territory; the panel is not the players.

---

## THE ONE THING AN OUTSIDER WOULD SAY MONDAY

Freeze the governance. It's good enough — *ALMOST* is right. Point the whole new apparatus at **shipping three games cleared through the new gate and put in front of real students this term**, and turn the accessibility gate into one external artifact (a workshop or a grant paragraph) that lets someone outside the studio validate it. The machine is ready. Now make it produce, before it produces more machine.

*Kept honest per house rule: this is the case a skeptical outsider would make. The internal counter-case — that the governance IS the product for an accessibility-first studio, and that portability and checks are what let one person operate at this range — is real too, and Matt is the one in the room to weigh it.*
