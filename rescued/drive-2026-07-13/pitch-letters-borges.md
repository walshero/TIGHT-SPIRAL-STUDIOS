I'd like to write a 900-word Ideas piece arguing that the hardest problems in AI-era knowledge work — infinite plausible text, archives too complete to think with, documentation nobody reads — aren't new. Borges catalogued every one of them in a handful of short stories, failure mode by failure mode, eighty years before the product cycle caught up.

## 2. MIT TECHNOLOGY REVIEW
‹SUBMIT VIA: ›

Subject: Pitch: Accessibility-first architecture — what a blind designer's pipeline gets right

I'd like to propose a piece on a design pattern with an unusual pedigree: a game studio run by a designer with retinitis pigmentosa, where every narrative title ships as a medium-agnostic scene graph — world as data, renderers separate — before any visual work begins. The result is that the voice-only version for blind players isn't a port; it's the same game through a different renderer. Accessibility as birthright, not retrofit.

The architecture is old MIT stock — it's how Zork modeled its world in the late 1970s — but the framing is literary: the studio's design rules map, one to one, onto problems Borges worked out in the 1940s, from branching state ("The Garden of Forking Paths") to verification under infinite plausible text ("The Library of Babel").

The angle for your readers: the engineering case that constraint-first design produces more durable systems, told through a working pipeline rather than a position paper. I can show the artifacts.

I'm a professor of English at MassBay Community College, serve on institutional AI governance, and built the studio's games as single-file, offline, fully accessible HTML.

## 3. HARVARD BUSINESS REVIEW
‹SUBMIT VIA: ›

Subject: Pitch: Why your AI team needs someone who reads

I'd like to propose a piece for your AI coverage making a talent argument most organizations are getting backwards: the humanist on an AI initiative isn't there to soften the engineering. They're there because the failure modes of total systems — infinite generated text, archives too complete to navigate, documentation that duplicates the work it describes — are old, well-documented problems, and the people trained on that documentation are humanists.

The evidence is a working case: I'm a professor of English who built a functioning production pipeline for accessible educational software, and every governing rule in it traces to problems Borges catalogued in the 1940s. The piece translates five of those rules into terms any team lead can apply Monday: verify in application before adopting; index knowledge as compressed kernels, not complete archives; give the all-seeing review exactly one question to answer.

The management takeaway: the recurring institutional error is buying unprecedented solutions to precedented problems. Pattern recognition on which is which is a hiring criterion, and it lives in the humanities.

I teach at MassBay Community College, serve on AI governance and faculty-adoption initiatives, and have twenty years of assessment-systems experience.

## 4. THE ATLANTIC (Technology)
‹SUBMIT VIA: ›

Subject: Pitch: Funes in the archive — what total memory is doing to institutions

Borges wrote a story about a man who remembers everything and is therefore incapable of thought. I'd like to write about what that story predicts for institutions in the AI era — because we are all building Funes now: complete transcripts, total archives, meeting recordings nobody replays, documentation so faithful it duplicates the labor of reading the thing it documents.

I watch this from inside a resource-constrained community college, where I teach English and serve on AI governance, and from inside a small studio I run building accessible educational games — where the survival rule became: keep the kernels, the small set of load-bearing truths beneath any standard, and let the rest go. Standards vary; kernels don't. Thinking, as Borges understood, requires forgetting.

The piece is part institutional reporting, part literary argument, part working case study — including the moment an AI confidently invented a departmental rubric that never existed, and what catching it taught us about verification. Roughly 2,000 words; a longer draft exists.
