# Tight Spiral Productions — Visual Constitution v1.1

*Timeless craft in the age of AI. The studio's spine for how everything looks, and why every object exists. Referenced by the OS as §13; lives standalone so the timing belt can cite it directly. Portable Markdown, no tool dependency.*

*Landed: 2026-07-04 (founder voice session). Fills the gap the belt was missing — game-design vision (Jenova flow, Maslow-is-CYL) had no rule tying it back to the studio face. This document is that rule.*

*Amended 2026-07-11 (founder design note): added §7.1 The header budget & the fold. NEEDS PHONE-CANONIZATION — the phone copy is truth.*

---

## 0. Why this exists

The belt moves builds and catches bugs. It did not check whether a build carries the studio's visual language. So games drifted from the studio face. This Constitution is what the belt now checks against.

Two things sit above the whole document and are never overridden: **no emoji, ever**, and **accessibility is a design floor, not an afterthought.** If any craft rule below ever conflicts with those two, those two win.

---

## 1. Purpose

TSP does not use AI to invent a new visual language. It uses AI to become a better traditional studio.

The goal is not to look "AI-generated." The goal is work that could plausibly have been produced by a thoughtful independent studio in the late twentieth century — exceptional craft, careful observation, iterative design.

**If a viewer notices AI before they notice the idea, the work has failed.**

---

## 2. Core principle

AI accelerates craftsmanship. AI does not replace it. Every image should feel authored, never manufactured. The studio remains the artist; AI is a production assistant.

---

## 3. The studio promise

Optimize for: clarity, memory, curiosity, play, systems thinking, accessibility, craft, replayability, knowledge.

Not for spectacle.

---

## 4. Every visual earns its place

Nothing exists only because it is attractive. Every object performs at least one meaningful function: teach, guide, foreshadow, reward observation, support navigation, tell a story, record history, measure progress, invite interaction, connect ideas.

If an object serves none of these, remove it.

---

## 5. The One Room principle

The studio is one coherent world, not a collection of pages. Every product, document, game, lesson, prototype, and experiment occupies a meaningful location inside the same world. Visitors learn the studio by exploring it. The world itself is the navigation system.

---

## 6. The timing belt is the operating system

The railroad is not decoration. It represents the loop: questions → research → ideas → prototypes → play → evidence → knowledge → better questions. Nothing skips the loop. Every project travels the same rails.

**6.1 The game-tie clause (the fix this document was written for).** A build is not "on the rails" merely because it ships. It is on the rails when its visual language ties back to the studio face — when the studio's mnemonic vocabulary (§8) actually appears in the thing that plays. A game's internal design vision must be legible in its surface:

- **Flow hierarchies (Jenova).** A build with a difficulty/mastery curve renders that curve as visible studio vocabulary — a switch track, a rising grade, a station sequence — not as an invisible parameter.
- **Maslow-is-CYL.** Choose Your Leader's need-hierarchy is a studio idea, not a CYL-only idea. Where a build stages human needs or motivation as a ladder, it uses the same visual ladder across builds so a returning player recognizes it.
- **The test:** open the build's design doc, then open the build. If the design vision is not visible on screen in studio vocabulary, the belt flags drift. The vision living only in the doc is the failure this clause exists to catch.

---

## 7. The 50% text gate (hard floor)

**No page or screen lands where text occupies more than 50% of visual space** — unless empty space is deliberately the point. Visual rhetoric, mnemonics, and engagement do the work leading industries expect of them; reading is what's left after the image has already taught.

This is a gate, not a preference. It rides in the PATTERN-TICK. A build failing it is not done.

Measured intent, not pixel-perfect audit: if a screen reads as a wall of text with decoration, it fails; if the image carries the idea and text confirms it, it passes.

**7.1 The header budget & the fold.** A splash is only a splash if it fills the first screen. A header or nav bar that eats a third or more of the viewport pushes the idea below the fold and kills the full-screen effect — the visitor lands on chrome, not content. Roomy is earned by the content, never spent on the header.

- **The budget:** on first paint, header/nav claims a lean share of viewport height and yields to the image-carried idea above the fold. The hero teaches in the first screen; the header orients without dominating.
- **Smallest viewport first:** design to the phone, then let it breathe up. A header that looks lean on a monitor can swallow a phone screen. Check phone, tablet, and desktop — the fold moves on every device.
- **The test:** load the page at a phone viewport. Is the studio's primary idea visible without scrolling? If the first thing under the browser bar is a fat banner and content starts below the fold, it fails.
- **Where it bites:** this is an authoring-time floor, held on every build and at the Studio Eyes gate. The unattended weekly guard cannot measure layout (it sees stripped markup, not the rendered fold), so this clause is enforced when the build is made, not by the link sweep. Honest teeth: named where they can bite, not where they can't.

## 8. Mnemonic architecture

Objects are memory devices. The vocabulary is consistent across games, sites, presentations, classrooms, docs, and products:

| Object | Means |
|---|---|
| water wheel | continuous improvement |
| bridge | connection between disciplines |
| switch track | decision point |
| station | knowledge category |
| tunnel | future work |
| lighthouse | orientation |
| spiral | reflection |
| timing belt | coordination |

Objects become vocabulary. The same symbols recur — that recurrence is what makes them mnemonic.

---

## 9. Catalog density

Inspired by classic illustrated worlds (the Richard Scarry depth-of-catalog the founder cites) — not because they are crowded, but because every revisit reveals something new. The world rewards attention. Hidden details teach; they do not merely entertain. **No wasted space — kernels of attention and knowledge fill the frame, unless empty space is itself the lesson.**

---

## 10. Quiet complexity

Complex systems, simple presentation. Every screen communicates one primary idea. Secondary discoveries emerge only after exploration. Deep, never overwhelming.

---

## 11. The logo

A circle where the **outer line is the circle** — the boundary itself, coiling inward. The mark is the tight spiral rendered literally: listening for feedback and coiling down toward the center. Not a spiral floating inside a ring; the ring *is* the outermost turn of the spiral.

---

## 12. Material rules

Every illustration should plausibly be producible with techniques available before widespread digital illustration: graphite, ink, brush, watercolor, gouache, colored pencil, cut paper, screen printing, letterpress, rub-on lettering, airbrush, technical drafting, architectural illustration, printmaking, cel animation, physical collage, hand-painted signage.

Traditional technique is a creative constraint, not nostalgia.

---

## 13. Prohibited aesthetics

Avoid: cinematic lighting, lens flares, depth-of-field effects, volumetric fog, hyperreal rendering, metallic gradients, plastic surfaces, algorithmic ornament, gratuitous texture, concept-art composition, "epic" fantasy framing, visual clutter without meaning, images designed primarily to impress.

---

## 14. AI usage policy

AI imitates historical production workflows, not contemporary AI aesthetics.

Preferred workflow: thumbnail sketches → composition studies → value studies → hand-crafted line work → limited color → print-era finishing → human review → iterative refinement.

HITL closes every asset (OS §11.11). Generator output is raw stock, never a ship.

---

## 15. Color philosophy

Restrained palettes. Color communicates hierarchy — do not color everything. Reserve strong color for: interaction, feedback, progress, danger, discovery, player agency.

**Reconciling with the arcade default.** Palette B (High Lumen amber, 16.1:1) is the legibility *floor* for games — the default-on legible state, comfort a knob never a gate. Color-for-meaning is the *discipline layered on top* of that floor. These are not in conflict: B guarantees the screen is readable on load; §15 governs what additional color is allowed to mean once it's readable. Confluence carries studio green because it is the institutional assessment tool, not a game — palette is a per-build call, never an inherited default.

---

## 16. Typography

Typography is illustration. Hand lettering is encouraged. Display type should feel built, not selected. Avoid fashionable fonts; prefer timeless readability. (Legibility floors from OS §11.17 still bind — built does not mean illegible.)

---

## 17. Interaction philosophy

Every click answers a question. Every interaction strengthens understanding. Feedback is visible; consequences are visible; knowledge moves. The interface rewards curiosity rather than demanding efficiency.

---

## 18. The living world

The homepage is not a dashboard — it is a living landscape. Visitors gradually understand where things are, how they connect, what is active, what has been discovered, where help is needed, what is unfinished, and how they can contribute.

---

## 19. Accessibility

Beauty without accessibility is incomplete design. Every visual decision improves contrast, legibility, touch targets, navigational clarity, multimodal understanding, symbol recognition, low-vision usability, screen-reader compatibility. Accessibility is part of the aesthetic.

---

## 20. The Brand Test

Every image passes five questions:

1. Can it be understood without reading?
2. Does every object earn its place?
3. Would this still feel intentional in a children's museum exhibit?
4. Would this still feel beautiful in twenty years?
5. Could a skilled illustrator have produced this in 1990?

Any "no" → revise.

---

## 21. Final principle

TSP does not celebrate artificial intelligence. It celebrates human curiosity. AI helps build worlds that invite better questions. Those questions — not the technology — are the heart of the studio.

---

## Belt integration (how this bites)

**VISUAL-TICK folds into PATTERN-TICK.** Before any page ships, the belt checks, in addition to the existing C6 / changelog / nav checks:

- **50% text gate** (§7) — does the image carry the idea, or is this a wall of text?
- **Header budget & fold** (§7.1) — at a phone viewport, is the idea above the fold, or does a fat header eat the splash?
- **Game-tie clause** (§6.1) — for any build with a design doc, does the design vision appear on screen in studio vocabulary?
- **Brand Test** (§20) — five questions, any "no" flags revise.

One graduated change, per the accumulation brake: the Constitution is the add; VISUAL-TICK extends the existing PATTERN-TICK rather than standing as a new beat.
