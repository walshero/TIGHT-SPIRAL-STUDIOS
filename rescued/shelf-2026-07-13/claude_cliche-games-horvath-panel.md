# Panelist Weigh-In: Horvath on the Cliché Games

*A simulated review of the learning mechanics in Cliché Field, Cliché Line, and Cliché City, argued from the published principles of learning scientist Jared Cooney Horvath ("How the Brain Learns"). This is the case Horvath's framework would make — not literal quotes from him. Tight Spiral Studios design note, 2026.*

---

## The one-line verdict

"You've built attention machines. The learning isn't in the shooting — it's in the split-second judgment you force before the shooting. Protect that judgment from everything else competing for the player's attention, and these work."

## Where the design already agrees with the science

**No memory without attention — and you gate attention well.** My first principle is that there is no learning without memory, and no memory without attention. The redesign that stripped the color-coding out of Cliché Field is the single best learning decision in the whole suite. The moment a tile *announced* itself as cliché-or-fresh by color, the player's attention went to the color, not the language — and you can't encode what you never attended to. Making every capsule identical parchment forces the eye onto the words. That is the learning event. Keep it.

**The force field in Cliché Line is textbook attention-gating.** Attention doesn't multitask; it switches, and every switch costs. Cliché Line's "field up — read the line, no firing" window is exactly right: it single-tasks the reading before it asks for the acting. Most educational games fail because they make you read *and* dodge *and* aim simultaneously, and the reading loses. You separated the channels in time. That's the design working with the brain instead of against it.

**The beam is a working-memory accommodation, not just a control.** Pulling a phrase in and holding it still to inspect it up close removes the motor load while the player does the cognitive work of judging it. For any learner — and especially one navigating a visually demanding field — that "stop and read one thing" affordance lowers extraneous load so more capacity goes to the actual discrimination. The hold-to-commit is a bonus: deliberate commitment is a deeper level of processing than a reflexive grab.

**Recognizing clichés IS pattern recognition, which is what expertise is made of.** Expertise is not knowing more facts; it's recognizing more patterns faster. A player who reads a few hundred phrases and repeatedly sorts "tired" from "alive" is building exactly the pattern-detector a working editor runs unconsciously. The games are, whether you framed them this way or not, a pattern-recognition trainer for prose.

**The joyful surprises are encoding aids, not decoration.** Novelty and mild emotional spikes bias the brain toward encoding whatever is present when they fire. The golden line, the cliché that cracks open and drops something alive — those aren't just delight, they're dopamine timestamps that make the surrounding moment stickier. Endorsed. One note: tie the surprise to the *content* (a cliché breaking into fresh language does this beautifully) so the memory hook attaches to the lesson, not just to the fireworks.

## Where I'd push back

**Difficulty should live in the judgment, never in the joystick.** The risk with any arcade frame is that the bottleneck quietly becomes reflex — and reflex speed is not what you want to train. Cliché Field's slow drift and Cliché City's *no timer at all* are correct instincts; guard them. If a player is losing because their thumbs are slow, you're testing the wrong thing. If they're losing because they misjudged "paradigm shift" as fresh, you're testing the right thing. Instrument for that difference.

**Recognition is easier than production — build the bridge deliberately.** Spotting a cliché in a floating tile is a recognition task. Not writing one in your own draft is a production task, and recognition does not automatically transfer to production. To earn the transfer, add one small generative beat: after a round, ask the player to *recall* one cliché they almost kept, or to *replace* a piece of pile-rubble with fresh language of their own. Even ten seconds of generation does more for transfer than another hundred tiles of recognition.

**Space it, don't cram it.** These are short by design, which is good — but three rounds today teaches far less than one round a day for three days. If this ever becomes a real classroom tool, the highest-leverage change is trivial: bring students back to it across sessions rather than in one sitting. Spacing is the closest thing learning science has to a free lunch.

**Watch extraneous load at the edges.** The wrap-around clipping, the pile, the rail, the confetti — each is fine alone, but text competes with motion for the same limited attention. The current builds are clean; keep them clean. Every non-essential moving thing on screen is borrowing from the budget the player needs to read.

## The highest-return next move

If you want these to *teach* and not just entertain, add the generative bridge — the "recall or replace" beat at the end of a round. It's the smallest change with the largest effect on whether the skill leaves the game and shows up in a student's actual sentences. Everything else here is already pulling in the right direction.

### Principle-to-mechanic map (quick reference)

| Horvath principle | Where it lives in the games |
|---|---|
| No memory without attention | Identical tiles force reading (Field); force-field read window (Line) |
| Attention switches, doesn't split | Read-phase vs. act-phase separated in time (Line); beam removes motor load while judging (Field) |
| Expertise = pattern recognition | Repeated cliché-vs-fresh sorting builds the detector (all three) |
| Desirable difficulty | Removing the color tell; untimed placement (City) |
| Novelty/emotion aids encoding | Golden line, cracked-cliché mote, blooms, confetti (Field) |
| Generation > recognition for transfer | **Gap — add a recall/replace beat** |
| Spacing | **Gap — space replays across sessions** |
| Timely, specific feedback | Pile, bruise message, recovery bloom, rail assembly |
