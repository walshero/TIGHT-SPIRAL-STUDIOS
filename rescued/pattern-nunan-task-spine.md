## 7. The Task Spine — production-in-role beats matching

**The rule (new; names a thing the studio already does):** a learning game earns its keep when the player *does a task the discipline would recognize as its work* — not when the player recognizes or matches an item. This is Nunan's pedagogical task (TBLT) folded into the studio's existing DNA (Gee's learning-by-doing, Osterweil's freedom-to-experiment, the Wireframe Novelty Gate). A task has a goal, real content, a process, and a **product** — and the product is either *open* (many valid outcomes) or *closed* (one). The open/closed fork is the design lever.

**Where it goes:** the parameters stage of every teaching build, sitting next to the Medium Gate and the Wireframe Novelty Gate. Before you pick art or mechanic, decide what the player *produces* and whether that product is open or closed. If the honest answer is "they pick the right one," you've drawn a quiz, and a quiz can't show transfer or carry house identity.

**Why it works.** Matching tests recognition, and recognition is recall wearing a costume. It reads the same whether the subject is English, biology, or accounting — so it can't prove transfer and can't carry the studio's voice. A task makes the player act *from a stance*, with real content, toward something a practitioner would name as the discipline's actual move. Text-matching is fine plumbing and stale as a spine: not discipline-distinctive, not evidence of transfer.

**The four mechanics, ranked by transfer they prove** (high to low):

1. **Roles** — the player *occupies* a stance (editor, peer responder, rater, reader) and acts from it. Strongest. It's the studio's defensible niche — already the spine of litmag-desk, Glass Engine, and avatar-builder ("the prop carries the role"). Roles force the discipline's real moves and produce an open product.
2. **Move things** — drag / sort / arrange where **position carries meaning**: sequence a revision, arrange evidence, build a paragraph. Tests the spatial reasoning the discipline genuinely uses. Pairs with the cut-paper tile library and the texture-not-color rule.
3. **Combo** — chain moves so **order matters**: respond globally then locally; apply the CRAP principles in sequence. Tests procedural knowledge, not item knowledge. layout-levels runs a version of this.
4. **Puzzles** — lowest of the four, highest risk of sliding back into matching. A puzzle with one closed answer *is* matching with extra steps. It only earns its place when the **solution path is the discipline's reasoning**, not the answer itself.

**The one-line test:** *A task beats a quiz when the player produces something — open or closed — that the discipline would recognize as its work.* Matching is closed + recognition. The studio's niche is production-in-role.

**Accessibility note:** none of the four mechanics may rely on color, fine motor precision, or hunting for a control. "Move things" especially — drag must always have a keyboard path (select-then-place), tap targets at the 44px floor, and position-meaning carried by label + texture, not hue. A role-based task narrated in low-vision-safe type beats a slick drag interaction the founder can't operate.

**Source:** Nunan, D. *Task-Based Language Teaching* (Cambridge UP, 2004) — open/closed task products and the task-as-goal-process-product frame. Reached the studio via TBLT/GBL design literature (Maghsoud; the Nunan task-design summaries). Attribute on use.
