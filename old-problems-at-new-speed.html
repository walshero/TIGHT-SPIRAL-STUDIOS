Old problems
at new speed.

In 1955, as his blindness became nearly total, Jorge Luis Borges was made director of a library of a million books — handed the volumes and the darkness in the same gesture. He kept working for thirty years, composing in his head.

The studio that built this piece is run by a professor with retinitis pigmentosa, who makes games a blind student can play as the game — not an accommodation bolted on. He did not set out to imitate Borges. But every load-bearing rule that made the pipeline work turned out to have been solved, as a problem, in a handful of short stories from the 1940s.

Here is the whole argument, so you carry it through every room: the hard problems of the AI era are not new. They are old problems at new speed. This piece gives you five of them — the way Hitchcock gives you the bomb under the table, so you sit in the knowing.

Scroll. Five rooms. Each one you already understand.

The Garden · total branchingEvery next, held at once.

"The Garden of Forking Paths" (1941) describes a novel that is also a labyrinth — a book in which every time a character faces alternatives, the story takes all of them at once. It is the opening selection of digital media's standard anthology. Which is the field's way of saying: this is where we begin.

You already know the trap: you will want to ask "what happens next?" The design question is never that. It is "what structure holds all the nexts at once?"

Choose. The garden keeps what you don't.

The engineering answer is the one Zork's authors found at MIT in the late 1970s: the world is data. Rooms and consequences live in a structure; the prose that describes them is a separate layer that reads the structure aloud. Story logic first, as a law — before a single expensive pixel. The studio's newest game ships as a playable, medium-agnostic scene graph before any art lane opens, so the blind player's version is the game.

Turkle, from the corner seat: a branch you were shown and did not take is still a road you now carry. Total systems are generous with roads and stingy with the hour you have to walk one.

The Library of Babel · total textEverything exists; nothing can be trusted.

"The Library of Babel" (1941) imagines a universe that is a library of every possible book — every true book, every false one, every refutation, and every catalog of the others, true and false, identically bound. The librarians are not empowered by this totality. They are ruined by it. The cost has merely moved from writing to verifying.

I know a more precise description of nobody's working life in 2026. One of these two cards is a lie. They are bound the same. You cannot tell by looking — that is the whole point. Only one move settles it.

Two catalog cards. One describes a rubric the department has never possessed.

Early in the studio's assessment work, an AI-drafted document confidently described a four-point argumentative rubric the department has never had. It was caught the only way such things are caught: checked against the actual documents, byte for byte. So the studio runs on a discipline the librarians never got — nothing is real until verified in application. Files carry checksums. Claims carry provenance: what source, what license, verified when, by whom. A confident answer inside an expert domain is a trigger to consult, not to proceed.

Provenance is the content. Where and from whom a thing was learned is not metadata — it is the primary unit of meaning, and the alternative to scraped, de-attributed text. The library is infinite. The shelf you can trust is the one you built.

Funes · total memoryHe remembered everything, and could think nothing.

"Funes the Memorious" (1942) gives us a young man who, after a fall, remembers everything — every leaf on every tree, every time he perceived it. Borges's cruel and correct observation: possessing total recall, Funes is thereby incapable of thought. Thinking requires abstraction, and abstraction requires forgetting — the collapse of a million particulars into one usable idea.

Above him is everything, kept. You can't read a word of it — that is what "kept everything" looks like. There is exactly one button worth pressing.

Total recall. No thought. The archive no one can navigate.

Every knowledge system that fails in higher education — and every one this founder has over-built — failed the Funes way. The complete archive no one can navigate. The documentation so faithful it duplicates the labor of reading the thing it documents. The AI transcript saved in full because deleting feels like loss. The studio's answer is a principle held as core: knowledge lives as kernels — the small set of load-bearing truths beneath any standard — identified, verified, indexed, folded to maximum density.

The kernel is what you keep. Funes is what happens when you keep everything else too. The accessibility standard was revised out of a guideline last year; the kernel beneath it — the physics of contrast, the research on legible letterforms — did not move.

The Aleph · total visionSee everything. Ask one thing.

"The Aleph" (1945) is a point in a Buenos Aires basement that contains all other points — look into it and you see the whole universe at once, from every angle. The man who owns the basement, a mediocre poet, uses this total vision to write an interminable poem describing the entire planet, acre by acre. Borges's narrator sees it too, is shattered — and then, the part that matters, goes back upstairs and gets on with his life.

You can look in. It will show you everything the studio holds, all at once. And then it will let you ask it exactly one question. The discipline is the whole gift.

A point on the cellar floor. It holds every file, every build, every stalled lane.

The one permitted question: Why aren't builds moving? Not "how do we perfect the system." Not the interminable poem. One diagnostic question — then upstairs. (The honest answer, this quarter: the studio does not have a building problem. It has a finishing problem. The essay this piece delivers is done, and unsent.)

Eagleman, on the stairs: a nervous system given everything at once does not perceive more — it seizes. Perception is what's left after the brain throws most of the signal away. The Aleph disciplined to one question is just a brain that learned to attend.

On Exactitude · total documentationA map the exact size of the empire.

Borges needed a paragraph for "On Exactitude in Science" (1946): an empire's cartographers, chasing perfection, produce a map the exact size of the empire, point for point. The following generations find it useless and abandon it to the weather.

Watch it happen. The more faithful the map becomes, the more worthless. You will want to perfect it. Then you will want to let it rot. Both instincts are correct.

One to one is perfect, and perfectly useless.

The studio's cross-session index is one page. It is one page on purpose, and keeping it one page is treated as maintenance of the same seriousness as fixing a bug — because the alternative, faithfully recorded, is the map rotting in the desert with the animals living in it. Every institution owns at least one such map.

Turkle again: the record that documents everything is often the one nobody reads — the meeting captured in full and replayed by no one. Fidelity is not the same as being known.

The turn.

It would be tidy to say Borges predicted computing. He did not, and the claim isn't needed. What he did was catalog, with terrible precision, the failure modes of total systems: total branching, total text, total memory, total vision, total documentation. Those are exactly the systems the industry now sells — and the failure modes arrive bundled with the license.

A humanist's training — the slow reading of old texts about knowledge, memory, and labyrinths — is a working inventory of which problems are genuinely new (few) and which are old problems at new speed (most). Not ethics as a garnish. Pattern recognition running about eighty years ahead of the product cycle.

Borges ran a national library he could not read, and did it by knowing, better than the sighted, how libraries actually fail. This studio's newest game is authored for the ear before the eye, by a designer whose eyes are the reason the floors exist. The person who cannot take the interface for granted is the one who finds out what the system is actually made of.

For chrissake — we have been assigning these stories for decades. We might also believe them.
