Headwaters · Why This Exists

Assessment starts where the work goes.

Every semester, student work leaves our classrooms and meets a real audience: a magazine reader, a podcast listener, a poster-session visitor, an industry professional at an expo. When work travels like that, something useful happens for assessment — the artifact, the audience, and the outcome are all visible in the same place.

Confluence is the department's tool for reading that evidence together. It is not a new requirement and not a new platform to log into. It is one file — this one opens the same way — that helps faculty and coordinators collect student work, norm as raters, score against shared outcomes, and hand a clean record to program review.

The shape of it

Picture the department as a watershed. Courses are the headwaters. Student work flows out through several channels — five of them are mapped below. They join at the confluence, where artifacts are read against learning outcomes. Downstream, the gauge measures how consistently we read, and the whole record feeds program review.

Five Tributaries · What Students Make

Work that already leaves the room.

None of these channels was invented for assessment. Each one exists because students wanted an audience. That is exactly what makes them strong evidence: the work is authentic, public, and finished to a standard an outside reader can judge.

The literary magazine

Audience: campus and the public · annual print issue and a public website

The college's student literary magazine has published creative writing, illustration, photography, and artwork from students, faculty, and staff since 2016, and now accepts film and music as well. Its issues have earned national recognition, including the National Council of Teachers of English REALM First Class rank and multiple Community College Humanities Association awards for individual student pieces.

Evidence it yields: finished creative work, revision history behind each accepted piece, and a student editorial process — reading, selecting, and designing — that is itself assessable.

The student podcast

Audience: listeners on cable access and online · student-produced episodes

Communication students launched a student-run podcast in partnership with a local cable access station. Episodes are created, written, produced, and edited by student interns, and podcasting now sits alongside social media and video production as a named internship track in the Communication program.

Evidence it yields: scripted and revised audio storytelling, interviewing, and editing — oral communication outcomes with a public artifact attached.

Honors poster sessions

Audience: the campus community, in the library · public presentation and Q&A

Honors sections of English Composition II close with a public poster session: students present their semester research as posters in the library and field questions from visitors. The poster distills a research question, sources, and an argument onto one surface a stranger can follow.

Evidence it yields: argument from evidence in a compressed public form, plus a live defense — the same skills the composition sequence assesses, performed for an audience.

Portfolios

Audience: the department · the classroom-to-review channel

Portfolios are the quietest channel and the widest: a curated body of a student's work across a course, carried forward for program-level reading. Where the other channels sample the strongest work, portfolios show range — drafts, revisions, and reflection alongside finished pieces.

Evidence it yields: growth over time and the revision outcome made visible, in the student's own arrangement.

The STEM Expo — the campus model

Audience: campus, industry, schools, and the public · in person and online

Twice a year the college's STEM Expo puts student projects — from autonomous robots to student-designed games — in front of the campus, local industry, and the public. An in-person session pairs with a week-long online exhibition where visitors watch short project videos and post questions, and presenters answer daily. Industry professionals have hired students after meeting them there.

Evidence it yields: artifact, explanation, and public response in one package. The Expo's format — make, show, respond — is the model this whole approach generalizes: every channel above is a version of it.

Five channels, one pattern: the work travels, an audience receives it, and the department can read what came back. The next page maps that reading onto outcomes.

The Confluence · Mapping Work to Outcomes

Where artifacts meet outcomes.

An artifact by itself is a nice thing. An artifact read against a shared outcome is evidence. The confluence is where the two meet: every channel's work can be read against the same five program outcomes, in plain language.

The five outcomes, plainly

1

Read carefully and critically. Students engage texts closely and question what they read.

2

Write with their own thinking. The voice and the ideas on the page belong to the student.

3

Build an argument from evidence.This year's focus Students weigh sources, put evidence to work, and deal honestly with the other side.

4

Revise and collaborate. Writing improves through drafts and through other readers.

5

Think about what language does. Students notice how language works on an audience — including their own.

How a channel reads against an outcome

Take this year's focus, argument from evidence. A poster session is almost a pure sample of it: research question, sources, claim, defense. A magazine essay shows it in finished prose. A podcast episode shows it in structure — what the episode chooses to include and in what order. A portfolio shows it developing across drafts. The Expo shows its STEM cousin: a project justified to a skeptical visitor.

The mapping runs upward, too. Course outcomes roll into program outcomes; program outcomes align with the college's graduation competencies and institutional outcomes. One artifact, read once, can inform every level — which is why collecting it well matters, and why the reading itself has to be trustworthy. That is the gauge's job.

The Gauge · How the Reading Cycle Runs

Trustworthy reading, on purpose.

The hard part of outcomes assessment has never been collecting work. It is knowing that two faculty readers scoring the same essay mean the same thing by a 3. The gauge is the annual cycle that makes that true — and Confluence's instruments exist for the two steps in the middle.

  1. Declare the cycle. Pick the outcome in focus and the courses contributing samples. One outcome per year keeps the reading deep instead of thin.
  2. Collect the samples. Work arrives from the channels — a student submits their own, or an instructor submits on their behalf. Names come off; the artifact stays.
  3. Norm the readers. Before scoring counts, every reader scores the same anchor samples. Confluence shows the group where readings agree and where they scatter, so the conversation happens before the data, not after.
  4. Score and converge. Readers score independently, then the group reviews the spread. Where scores diverge, readers talk until the divergence is understood — resolved, or documented as a real disagreement about the rubric.
  5. Report what was learned. The output is short: what the outcome looked like in student work this year, where readers agreed, what the department will change.
  6. Feed program review. Each annual cycle becomes one layer in the longer review record — five thin, honest layers instead of one scramble every five years.

Nothing in the cycle requires new software on anyone's machine. The scoring surfaces are files like this one — they open, they work offline, and the record they produce travels as ordinary documents.

Put-In · How to Contribute

Three ways in, none of them heavy.

A put-in is where a boat enters the river. There are three of them here, sized from an hour to a semester.

Send work downstream

The lightest entry: contribute student work samples from a course you teach. Two ways — students submit their own work they're proud of, or an instructor submits on a student's behalf with permission. Either way the sample joins the year's collection for the outcome in focus.

Join a norming session

One session, about an hour: score the anchor samples alongside colleagues and see the group's spread. This is the fastest way to understand what the tool does, because the calibration conversation is the product. No preparation needed beyond reading the samples.

Run the cycle in your own area

The full loop — declare, collect, norm, score, report — works for any program with artifacts and readers, not just English. The channels differ by discipline; the gauge does not. Coordinators who want to pilot a cycle can start from this file and the department's assessment guide.
