The Studio, Played

How one humanist mind reads a hard problem through eleven lenses — and an invitation to make MassBay the first pilot.

0 of 11 lenses

Most people meet one problem with one tool. I meet it with a room full of ways of seeing, and I pick the ones the problem is actually asking for.

My father is an electrician. I teach writing. I design games and I build assessment. Those aren't separate careers — they're lenses on the same system. This tour walks you through eleven of them, one at a time, and lets you play the pieces where I've already put them to work. At the end: what I think we could build together at MassBay.

The through-line: for two years my AI-literacy work has mostly been keeping English faculty from losing their minds. With ten days of quiet after the semester, I finally pulled together the pieces I'd been too buried to see — and this is the clearest way I've found to show them.

How work actually moves: the pipeline

A studio needs a spine or the ideas pile up and rot. Mine borrows from stage-gate engineering (the same discipline that ships cars and drugs) but bends it to protect the one scarce resource: human attention.

Every stage runs the same move: a machine clears the mechanical work → a human makes the judgment → the result is emitted. The machine never judges. It just clears the desk so my attention lands only on meaning.

Five outcomes at every gate — because go/stop is too blunt

Go — meets the bar, advance.
Conditional go — advance on one named fix.
Recycle — send back one stage; the idea's still alive.
Park — real, but not now; leaves with an un-park condition.
Kill — stop, and say why.
Pivot — the one-sentence answer to "what is this?" just changed; re-spine before anything else.

Grounded in Gee's learning loop, stage-gate literature (Cooper), and Osterweil's four freedoms — not generic production orthodoxy. The provenance is part of the point: every rule names where it came from.

Every piece carries its own receipt

You may have noticed the small brown lines: how this was made, where the idea came from, what a human decided. That's not decoration. It's a discipline I call human-in-the-loop-visible: the studio never hides the algorithm, it labels the move.

For a college weighing AI, that matters more than any single tool. The question isn't "did a machine help make this?" It's "can you see exactly where, and who was accountable?" Every artifact here answers that on its own face.

This is the same principle I bring to the AI Task Force: label the moves, keep a human at every gate, never let provenance drift into a false promise.

The reason I built this for MassBay

An invitation to make MassBay the first pilot

I've been building calibration and practice tools that I believe could move the needle on retention — the thing we all actually lose sleep over. I'd rather prove that at home than anywhere else.

What I'm proposing, at the smallest honest scale

  • A calibration pilot. Run one course's placement or portfolio scoring through the norming engine (Confluence) — prove inter-rater reliability improves, on real MassBay work, de-identified to FERPA standard.
  • A practice layer for retention. Build a small set of low-stakes practice materials — the kind of repeated, playful reps that keep a struggling student in the seat through week four.
  • A shared read on the IP. From the teaching-and-learning side, and from IT / governance — I want your eyes on how this should be protected, licensed, and (if it earns it) scaled.

What I'm asking

Teaching & Learning: a read on where this fits the CTL's retention and faculty-development priorities, and whether one course could host the pilot.

IT & governance: a read on data handling, FERPA lanes, and IP protection — and whether the institution has an appetite to be the proving ground rather than the last adopter.

No budget ask in this document. This is an invitation to look and to talk. If it's worth a next step, the next step is one conversation.
