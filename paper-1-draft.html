The Humanist's Pipeline

How a writing teacher built a working educational-technology system without writing code

Matt Walsh · Tight Spiral Studios · a draft, June 2026

Read me like this
You don't need any background. Start in the room above. Read straight through. When you hit a gray tool, open it and actually press the buttons — that's the whole point, the proof sits inside the paper. Tell me where you got lost or bored. That's the only feedback I need on a draft.

I am an English professor. I have spent twenty years inside the machinery of assessment at a community college — running portfolio readings, sitting on the boards, helping norm the scores that decide where a student starts. I am not an engineer. The last time I wrote code before this spring, it was a Dungeons & Dragons game in third grade, in DOS, on a friend's computer. I have always treated not being a programmer as a kind of superpower: it means I start from the problem a person actually has, not from the tool that happens to exist.

This is the story of what happened when I stopped waiting for someone to fix the machinery and built a piece of it myself — in days, not years — by using everything I already knew about how people learn, and using AI not as a ghostwriter but as a crew.

The thing I'd been told was impossible

For most of my career, the systems I work in have quietly shut people out. I have a degenerative eye condition, so I notice it more than most: tools that pop a menu somewhere I can't see it, screens that bury the one thing you need. For twenty years I assumed fixing that was someone else's job, someone with the credential I don't have.

What changed this spring is that I found a way to make the computer do the part I couldn't — the code — while I kept doing the part I'm actually good at: knowing what a learner needs, and refusing to ship anything that fails the person who has the hardest time using a screen. Accessibility stopped being a feature I begged for and became the floor I built on.

The constraint was never the problem. The constraint was the design. Build for the person who struggles most to see the screen, and the thing gets better for everyone.

I didn't use AI the way people are afraid of

The fear in every faculty meeting is the same: students will let the machine do the thinking. I share it. So I built the opposite. Instead of asking AI one question at a time, I gave it a cast of reviewers — named roles, each watching for one kind of mistake. One reads like a tired skeptic and asks whether something actually makes sense. One checks every fact. One guards accessibility. One watches whether the very first screen pulls you in or bores you. Several of them can stop a build cold until a problem is fixed.

None of them outranks me. They surface problems; I decide. It is the same instinct as a writing workshop — a room of readers, each catching what the others miss — except the room is always available and never tired. The machine doesn't think for me. It's made to show its work, so I can judge it the way I'd judge a student's.

What it built — go try it

Two examples are sitting right inside this page. Not screenshots. The actual tools. Open them.

Exhibit A — a five-minute game
Choose Your Leader

You read a real thing a president said, decide how much you trust them — then the game shows you what the original audience never got to see, and lets you feel how far the words moved you first. It teaches one habit: notice the gap between what was said and what was true.

The second isn't a game — it's a working piece of the assessment machinery I wanted on review day. A teacher drops in an assignment; the tool walks the logic out loud — what it teaches, how it's assessed, which rubric — and shows where the pieces line up before a single student paper is scored. It's the on-ramp; the real scoring system it feeds is called Confluence.

Paste any assignment. Watch it follow the river — the same logic I'd walk a colleague through — and show its reasoning at every step. Nothing hidden, nothing leaves the page.

Why this is the argument, not just the story

Here is the claim I'd defend to anyone: a person with no engineering background, working alone, built functioning, accessible educational technology in days — because the hard part was never the code. The hard part is knowing what a learner needs and being honest about whether the thing delivers it. That knowledge is what twenty years of assessment work gives you, and it is exactly what the big platforms, for all their engineers, keep getting wrong.

Education has been renting its judgment from vendors who profit from complexity. We don't have to. The tools above run on a single web page, work without internet, keep every scrap of data on your own device, and cost nothing to copy. If I can build them, a department can. A college can. The power was never held by the people who can code. It's held by the people who understand the problem.

This is the whole of it: the machine does the typing; the human keeps the judgment; the work shows its reasoning; and the person who struggles most to use it comes first.

That's the lead paper. There are three more behind it — one on the method (the cast of reviewers), one on the assessment architecture (the tool above and where it feeds), one on the game above and how it carries real weight without ever lecturing. But this is the door. If the room on review day made sense to you, and the tools worked in your hands, then you understand the thing I couldn't explain out loud for the last ten days. The vocabulary came after the building. The thing was always real.

Three questions, that's all
1. Did the opening room pull you in, or feel like homework? 2. Did the tools work when you opened them — and did playing them make the argument land? 3. Where did you get lost or bored? Mark the spot. Everything else I can fix.

A note on why this paper has a human in it

This draft was built with the same system it describes — an AI doing the typing, a human keeping the judgment. At the end of the night I made, I'll admit it, the obvious mistake: I asked the machine to keep running the loop while I slept, to have more finished by morning. It declined. Not because it couldn't, but because the whole point of what I built is that the loop does not close without a person in it. A system that grades its own work against its own claims, unwatched, overnight, is exactly the thing this studio exists to refuse. The human-in-the-loop is not a safety feature bolted onto the side. It is the load-bearing wall. Take the human out and you don't have a faster version of the method — you have the very thing the method was built against.

The same principle is why the work is better for being slow, and why I am writing this with thanks rather than alone. To my first reader — a paper that can't be understood by the person it's handed to has failed before it starts; you are the test the writing has to pass, and your confusion is more useful to me than any praise. And to Jeanie — the trauma-informed lens you've spent a career building is not a topic in this work, it is the floor under it; the rule that the maker is a person to be protected, not a machine to be optimized, is yours before it was ever mine.
