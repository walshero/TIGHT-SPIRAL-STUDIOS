Landing One

The Compass

Before the workshops, the reading, or the calendar, there is one question worth keeping on your desk: what is this seminar for? Everything in the trove points back to these outcomes. When a week gets away from you, teach toward one of these and you are still on course.

True North

Students will demonstrate the ability to use the wealth of academic, cultural, and social resources at MassBay for self-advocacy and individual empowerment.

The living learning outcome carried into the current FYS-designed courses.

Transition

Students make a successful move into college by taking responsibility for their learning and joining the MassBay community in a real, not nominal, way.

Self-Advocacy

Students learn to name what they need and to ask — from the right person, at the right desk, before the deadline instead of after it.

Belonging

On a commuter campus, connection is not automatic. The seminar builds the web of people — peers, faculty, staff — that keeps a student enrolled.

Academic Practice

Students read critically, enter an academic conversation with integrity, and treat research as a habit rather than a one-time assignment.

Landing Two

The Workshop Vault

Ten workshops that ran across three linked sections and held up. Each is a self-contained hour: an essential question, a few moves, and a note on who leads it. Run them in order for a full arc, or pull one when a Wednesday needs a spine. Nothing here needs a budget — it needs a room and a reason.

Workshop 01

Who Are We, and Why Are We Here?

Essential question: what did we each come here to do?

All hands on deck. Sections meet as one community, run introductions with stakes, and name a personal "why." Sets the social contract for the semester.

Lead: full FYS team + peer advisors
Workshop 02

Telling Your Story: The Power of Narrative

Essential question: how does a moment of change become an essay?

A master class on narrative — describe a significant person, place, or event and find its interior shape. Seeds a shared glossary: thesis, purpose, audience, evidence.

Lead: writing faculty
Workshop 03

What It Takes to Be a Successful Student

Essential question: what do students who make it actually do?

A student panel plus academic services. Real strategies from people a semester or two ahead. The single most-valued workshop in the pilot survey.

Lead: student panel + Academic Services
Workshop 04

Entering the Academic Conversation

Essential question: what does it mean to join a conversation instead of copying it?

Research, citation, and integrity in the digital age. Frame plagiarism as a principle, not a rule — it took the OneBook author ten years to research and write.

Lead: writing faculty + librarian
Workshop 05

Don't Believe Everything You Read

Essential question: where does a good source come from?

Hands-on with the library and academic databases. Build the search habit early so it is muscle memory by the research paper. Pair with a real assignment.

Lead: librarian
Workshop 06

What Stresses Us Out

Essential question: who do you call before it becomes a crisis?

A counseling-led panel on mental health, stress, and the family and health pressures the common reading raises. Names the people and the door before a student needs them.

Lead: Student Development / counseling
Workshop 07

Everyday Diversity and Belonging

Essential question: who belongs here, and how do we make that true?

Affirming difference in the pursuit of academic excellence. A curriculum on othering and belonging — the current FYS keeps this near the center, not the margin.

Lead: faculty facilitator
Workshop 08

The OneBook Kickoff

Essential question: what does it change when we all read the same thing?

Launch the shared text as a campus event. One book gives three sections — and a whole cohort — a single conversation to argue inside of all semester.

Lead: OneBook committee + FYS team
Workshop 09

Advising: MassBay and Beyond

Essential question: why do students leave — and why do they stay?

The honest talk about withdrawals, incompletes, GPA, financial aid, and transfer. Advising is the intervention that keeps students enrolled. Bring a peer advisor.

Lead: advising + peer advisors
Workshop 10

Where Are You Going; Where Have We Been?

Essential question: what did this semester make possible?

A reflective close — often over lunch. Students look back on the arc and forward to the next term. You gather the feedback that plans next year's series.

Lead: full FYS team

Three modern add-ons the field is asking for

The June retreat and recent teaching surfaced three workshops worth adding to the bank: AI Literacy — using and citing AI honestly in first-year work; Real Talks — a structured, low-stakes conversation practice that builds the peer trust everything else depends on; and a slowed-down Peer-Review Workshop that treats audience as the motivator it is. Draft these once, and they join the vault.

Landing Three · from the June 16, 2026 retreat

The Skills Constellation

The whole-group question at the retreat was simple: what will help our students succeed in college and after? The answers filled a wall. Grouped into six constellations, they become teachable — a set of stars to steer a seminar by. Every workshop in the vault lights up at least one of these.

Self-Command

  • Time management: how long will this actually take?
  • Executive function and meeting deadlines
  • Accountability for one's own learning
  • Bringing what you need to class

Know Thyself

  • Strengths and self-awareness
  • Identifying values
  • Naming your "why" for being here
  • Intrinsic motivation

Navigation

  • What resources exist and how to reach them
  • Just-in-time advising and information
  • Career interests and trajectory
  • Reading the map of the college

Voice

  • Self-advocacy and confidence
  • Talking with professors; writing email
  • Communicating across teaching styles
  • Asking before it is too late

Belonging

  • Building relationships and trust
  • Peer connection on a commuter campus
  • A sense of belonging you can feel
  • Community as a reason to stay

The Examined Mind

  • Challenging assumptions
  • Transfer of skills and knowledge
  • Learning how and why you failed
  • AI literacy; enduring what you don't love
Landing Four

The Common Reading

The engine of the whole model is embarrassingly simple: everyone reads one book. One shared text turns separate sections into a cohort with something to argue about. In the pilot it was Rebecca Skloot's The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks — a book that pulls research ethics, family, health, and identity into a single semester-long conversation. A strong common assignment turns the reading into a debate: for Skloot, is the Lacks family entitled to profits from the sale of HeLa cells?

Open the welcome-letter template (paste and edit)
Greetings from MassBay — we hope your summer is going well. We are writing to remind you that the writing course you have enrolled in is part of the First-Year Seminar program at MassBay. This is a chance to get to know the college and your fellow students, and to build the skills you will need to succeed here and beyond. We designed FYS to create a network of support — a web of connections to other students, faculty, and staff. Alongside your regular class meetings, you will take part in weekly workshops that bring several writing classes together. Those workshops range from finding your way around college resources to strengthening academic skills to managing time and stress. This year the college has adopted [COMMON BOOK / AUTHOR] as its OneBook. We will read it together across our sections. Research is consistent on this: students who take part in learning communities like this one are far more successful over the course of their college careers. We are glad you are in ours. See you in [MONTH]. — The First-Year Seminar Team

Choosing the text (an accessibility and ethics note)

Favor a common reading that is high quality, widely available, and — where possible — openly or affordably licensed, so cost is never the reason a first-year student falls behind on day one. Weigh the book's reach and impact, not just its reputation. Provide an accessible edition (screen-reader friendly, audio where you can) as a default, not an afterthought.

Landing Five

The Calendar Engine

A shared seminar lives or dies on rhythm. The pilot ran on a rhythm you can copy without inventing anything: a standing team meeting, a weekly workshop, and a common calendar that keeps three sections breathing together. It is a spine, not a straitjacket — your own class sessions fill the space between the beats.

Set the summer beats

A few summer planning meetings do most of the work: pick the top ten workshops and their order, draft the welcome letter, stand up a shared Blackboard/LMS space, and agree on common syllabus language for outcomes.

Protect the standing meeting

A short weekly team meeting is the cheapest insurance the program has. It is where the next workshop gets confirmed and where a struggling student gets flagged before the withdrawal.

Leave the wildcard open

Build the arc in units — narrative to open, the common book to anchor, an exit to close — and leave one unit as a wildcard the team shapes to the cohort in front of them.

Landing Seven · from the June 16, 2026 small-group work

The Expansion Frontier

The retreat's second question looked forward: what should an expanded FYS become, and how do we bring it to our online first-year courses? Here are the ideas the room generated, planted as flags and sorted by how quickly you could raise them. Start with the near shore.

Near shore — try this year

A faculty discussion board

A standing space where FYS faculty swap what worked this week. Low cost, high return, already possible in the LMS.

Owner: FYS team

A shared resource space

One folder or hub — this trove included — so no one rebuilds a workshop that already exists.

Owner: coordinator

More student voices

Students promoting FYS and the skills it builds, at orientation and in class. The pilot data says visibility is the gap.

Owner: peer advisors

FYS faculty at Orientation

Put FYS instructors in front of students and families at orientation so the program has a face before day one.

Owner: FYS team + Student Affairs

Open water — build next

Interactive online FYS

Design the seminar experience for online first-year courses — video resources, asynchronous community, real interaction, not just posted files.

Owner: online faculty + ID support

Expand the faculty pool

Grow beyond the writing sequence. Combine classes and create chances to collaborate across sections.

Owner: department + coordinator

A common reading, program-wide

Bring back a shared OneBook as the connective tissue across every FYS section.

Owner: OneBook committee

Workshop incentives

Recognize or compensate the work of building and leading workshops, so the effort is sustainable.

Owner: administration

External partnerships

Partner with groups beyond the college to widen what a workshop can offer.

Owner: coordinator

Deep sea — decisions for the task force

Require FYS within X credits?

A policy question: should every student meet FYS within a set number of credits? Weigh against "credit creep."

Owner: standards + task force

A dedicated course alongside the model?

Should a standalone seminar (for example, a PS 150) sit beside the embedded approach, or replace it?

Owner: curriculum committee

Measure the reach

Pull the number: what percentage of students take at least one FYS course? You cannot expand what you cannot see.

Owner: coordinator + IR
Landing Eight

The Quick-Start Lantern

New to FYS this term and short on time? Do not plan the whole voyage tonight. Take the next action, not the next project. Five moves put a working seminar in front of your students.

Pick your true north

Choose one outcome from the Compass to anchor the semester. Write it into your syllabus in plain language. One sentence is enough.

Pull three workshops from the Vault

Open with "Who Are We and Why Are We Here?", anchor the middle with a research or belonging workshop, and close with "Where Are We Going." You now have an arc.

Send the welcome letter

Paste the template from the Common Reading landing, drop in your book and month, and send it before the term starts. First contact before day one changes day one.

Set one standing meeting

If you are teaching alongside others, protect a short weekly team meeting. If you are solo, protect fifteen minutes with yourself to flag students who are drifting.

Book the closing reflection

Put the week-fifteen reflection on the calendar now. It gathers the feedback that plans next year — and it gives students an ending worth remembering.
