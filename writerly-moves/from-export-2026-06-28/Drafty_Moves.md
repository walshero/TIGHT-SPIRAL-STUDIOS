Drafty moves 

1. Overpacked Suitcase

What it flags: Details that may be weighing the scene down without adding enough value.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Long description where nothing new is learned
	•	Repeated info the reader already knows
	•	Side facts that never matter again
	•	Reader test: If you “unpack” this sentence/paragraph and leave it out, does anything important break—plot, character, mood?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Overpacked Suitcase → This patch might be carrying more detail than the story needs right now.

⸻

2. Shaky World Rules

What it flags: A crack in the internal logic of the story’s world.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	A character can suddenly do something they couldn’t before
	•	Magic/technology/time/physics works differently than it did earlier
	•	Social rules or constraints vanish when they’re inconvenient
	•	Reader test: Does this moment seem to ignore or rewrite a rule the story already taught me?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Shaky World Rules → Feels like the rules of this world are bending or breaking here.

⸻

3. Floating Camera

What it flags: Confusing POV or an ungrounded sense of space and time.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	You don’t know where characters are in relation to each other
	•	POV jumps without warning
	•	We “teleport” between locations with no clear transition
	•	Reader test: Can I say where we are, who’s here, and whose head I’m in?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Floating Camera → I lost track of where we are or whose eyes I’m seeing through.

⸻

4. Blurry Stakes

What it flags: Unclear what matters in this moment—what’s wanted, what’s risked.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Lots of activity, but you don’t know what “winning” or “losing” would look like
	•	Conflict that feels vague, low-impact, or interchangeable
	•	Reader test: Could I answer: “What does the character want right now, and what happens if they don’t get it?”
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Blurry Stakes → I’m not sure what’s on the line here for the character.

⸻

5. Dropped Thread

What it flags: Setups that seem important but never get pulled through (at least yet).
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Hinted secrets, objects, or tensions that disappear
	•	A side character introduced with weight, then forgotten
	•	Questions raised and never revisited
	•	Reader test: Did the story teach me to care about this thing, then let it vanish?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Dropped Thread → This element felt significant, but I don’t see it tying back in (yet).

⸻

6. Emotional Static

What it flags: Emotional beats that feel off—muted, overblown, or unearned.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Huge reaction with little buildup on the page
	•	Big event with almost no emotional response
	•	Tone of the emotion doesn’t match what just happened
	•	Reader test: Does the character’s emotional response feel both specific and proportionate to the moment?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Emotional Static → The feelings here don’t quite line up with what I’ve seen so far.

⸻

7. Stock Footage Moment

What it flags: A beat that feels generic, like borrowed movie stock instead of this specific story.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Familiar phrases (“heart pounding,” “frozen in fear,” “suddenly she woke up”)
	•	Predictable reaction or image where more surprising specificity could go
	•	A scene that plays like a common trope with no twist or personal angle
	•	Reader test: Could I swap this line/beat into a dozen other stories and it would still fit?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Stock Footage Moment → This feels more like default language or action than something unique to your story or character.

⸻

8. Fast-Forward Scene

What it flags: Moments where the story seems to skip over the good stuff too quickly.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Major decisions or emotional turns happen in one line
	•	We jump from setup to outcome without seeing any struggle, process, or lived detail
	•	Reader test: Did I want to watch how this happened, not just get told that it happened?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Fast-Forward Scene → This feels like an important beat that might deserve more time, texture, or steps.

⸻

9. Noticeable Info Dump

What it flags: A block of explanation that calls attention to itself and slows the story’s pulse.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Paragraphs of backstory or world history in the middle of live action
	•	Dialogue that turns into a lecture or data transfer
	•	The urge to skim to “get back” to what’s happening now
	•	Reader test: Does this chunk feel like the story hit pause to give me a briefing?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Noticeable Info Dump → This information might work better spread out, embedded in action, or trimmed.

⸻

10. Convenient Coincidence

What it flags: Plot turns that feel too neat, like luck or author intervention rather than earned consequence.
	•	Typical signs:
	•	Help arrives at the perfect moment with no cost
	•	The exact clue/object/person needed just appears
	•	A tough problem melts away without sacrifice or complication
	•	Reader test: Does this turn feel driven by earlier choices and setups, or did it just drop in from the sky?
	•	How a reader might use it:
Label: Convenient Coincidence → This solution/turn feels a bit too tidy; maybe it could be seeded earlier or made more costly.

⸻

Here’s a simple 2-column sheet you can drop into Docs/Word and print:

Drafty Move	How a reader can use this label
Overpacked Suitcase	Tag spots where description or info feels heavier than it needs to be. Ask: If we cut this, would anything important break—plot, character, or mood?
Shaky World Rules	Mark any moment that seems to break the story’s own logic or rules. Ask: Does this contradict something the story already taught me?
Floating Camera	Use when you lose track of where we are, who’s present, or whose POV we’re in. Ask: Can I clearly picture this scene and viewpoint?
Blurry Stakes	Flag scenes where you don’t know what the character wants or what’s at risk. Ask: What would “winning” or “losing” look like here?
Dropped Thread	Mark setups that seem important but then vanish (at least for now). Ask: Was I taught to care about this, only to have it disappear?
Emotional Static	Use when the feelings don’t quite match the moment (too big, too small, too sudden). Ask: Does this reaction feel specific and proportional?
Stock Footage Moment	Tag lines or beats that feel generic or cliché instead of specific to this story. Ask: Could this appear in a dozen other stories unchanged?
Fast-Forward Scene	Use when an important decision or event feels rushed or skimmed over. Ask: Did I want to see more of how this happened, not just that it did?
Noticeable Info Dump	Mark blocks of explanation or backstory that slow things down. Ask: Does the story feel like it hit pause to brief me?
Convenient Coincidence	Tag turns that feel too lucky or tidy, not earned by earlier choices. Ask: Does this feel caused by what came before, or dropped in from the sky?
