The book by the stairs

A worn paperback someone left on the landing. Somebody underlined this part.

A need, once it is met, stops being a motive. The person moves up and forgets it was ever a question. A need that is not met does not wait politely. It moves to the front and it stays there, and everything above it goes quiet — not because it stopped mattering, but because there is no room.

Paraphrase, not quotation. Maslow, A Theory of Human Motivation, 1943 — public domain (US, pre-1964 without renewal). Full text: Psychological Review 50(4).

Nothing in the book unlocks anything. It is here because it was on the landing.

You stopped here.

That is a real ending. You are not required to go all the way down to have understood something.

The point was never to reach the bottom. It was to notice the floor.

About

Every cell in the honeycomb is the same cell on every floor. The workplace does not change. The room with the train set does not change. The people you do not understand do not change.

What changes is how far away they are.

The cells never dim and never blur. Reducing legibility as a mechanic is a floor violation in this studio, and it is also a lie: scarcity does not make the good life invisible. It leaves it in plain view and puts it out of reach.

Theory in the design, not in the scene: Maslow (the floors); Festinger (what a person does when the record and the answer disagree); Gitlin (what coverage does to a story in motion); Herman & Chomsky (why the coverage arrives pre-shaped); Hall (dominant, negotiated, oppositional — the reader is not passive); Hartman (what the record still refuses to hold). None of them are quoted here. Their books are not open-license. They are the instrument, not the content.

Exposure model: graduated, never flooding; the player sets the pace; stopping is an ending. Drawn from VA/VRET practice, where immersion is the treatment and pacing is the patient's.

Slice status: one floor set, three cells, one task. LBJ 1964 and the record-turn are specified and next.
