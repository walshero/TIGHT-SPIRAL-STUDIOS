What I've been building

The plain version is right here. If you want to go deeper, every section has a “tell me more” you can open.

If you've talked to me lately and weren't sure what I meant, that's fair — I skipped the explaining and went straight to the building. Here's the whole thing in plain language.

I use AI the way a director uses a film crew. Not one assistant answering one question — a whole team that pressure-tests every idea before I approve it. I make every real decision. They catch my mistakes early.

I've been using that team to build small educational games — the kind a student plays in five minutes that teaches them something real, and that works for people with disabilities from the very first screen.

That's it. Everything else is just detail inside that one idea. Below, I've written the same explanation three ways, depending on who's asking.

The same thing, three ways

If you're a friend

You know how I get obsessed with a project. This one stuck. I figured out how to make AI act like a full creative team instead of a single chatbot — a writer, a designer, a critic, someone who keeps it honest. I tell them what I want, they argue it out and flag the weak spots, and I decide. I've used it to build little teaching games. It's the most fun I've had making things in years.

If you're a colleague who's skeptical of AI

I share the skepticism, which is exactly why I built it this way. The system never decides anything — it's built so a human (me) approves every meaningful choice, and so it has to show its reasoning instead of hiding it. It's the opposite of “let the AI write it for you.” It's “make the AI show its work, then judge it like you'd judge a student's.” The games it helps me build are accessible-first and grounded in real learning theory, not gimmicks.

If you're an administrator or on a committee

I've built a repeatable system for producing accessible educational technology with AI, where human judgment stays in control at every step and the work is documented as it goes. It's portable — it doesn't depend on any one vendor or subscription, and it runs offline with no student data leaving the device. It's directly relevant to faculty AI adoption, accessibility compliance, and responsible-use governance, because it's a working model of how to use these tools without surrendering judgment to them.

It's a cast of reviewers, each watching for one kind of mistake

Instead of asking AI one thing at a time, I gave it a set of named roles it plays when reviewing my work. One reads like a tired skeptic and asks “does this actually make sense?” One checks every fact. One guards accessibility — can someone with low vision actually use this? One watches that the very first screen pulls you in instead of boring you. A few of them have the power to stop a build cold until a problem is fixed.

None of them outranks me. They surface problems; I make the call. The point isn't to automate the decision — it's to make sure the obvious mistakes get caught before I waste a day on them. It's the same instinct as a writing workshop: a room of readers, each catching what the others miss.

I keep the group small for small jobs and convene the whole room only for things that matter — like anything a real student would touch.

There's a fixed order, so nothing important gets skipped

Every build follows the same path. First I decide whether the idea is even worth doing now, or whether it should wait. Then I name the one surprising thing the game does that I haven't seen done before — if there isn't one, it goes back. Then I pick how it should look. Then I build the smallest version that actually works, before polishing anything. Then I play it and fix what's broken — and fix the related things the one problem points to.

Each step has a gate: it doesn't move forward until it's actually ready, by a standard I wrote down ahead of time, not by whether it “feels” done in the moment. And if the whole idea of the game changes mid-build — which happens — that trips a stop, and I re-state what the game now is on a single screen before building further. That one rule alone has saved me from a lot of wasted motion.

It's not a feature I add at the end — it's the foundation

I have a degenerative eye condition, so I know firsthand how most software quietly shuts people out. So in everything I build, accessibility is a hard floor, not a nice-to-have: large readable type at full contrast, one decision per screen so nothing gets lost, big tap targets, full keyboard control, and a starting point that lands you somewhere clear instead of burying you in menus.

This isn't charity-mode design. Building for the person who has the hardest time using a screen makes the thing better for everyone. The constraint is a design advantage, not a tax.

The industry builds games faster with AI. I build games that are about something.

Most studios using AI this way are racing to make game assets — art, levels, code — faster and cheaper. That's real, and it's not what I'm doing. My system is aimed at a different target: making games that teach something true, where real thinkers in a subject shape the design, and where you can actually measure whether the learning happened.

It's also unusual who's doing it. I'm an English professor, not an engineer. I built this from a standing start, and it turns out it lines up closely with what the most advanced people in this space are doing — I just got there by following the teaching problem instead of the technology. The vocabulary came after the building, which is why it can be hard to explain. The thing is real; I just skipped the part where you learn the words first.

Small, playable, and pointed at one real skill each

A few examples, in plain terms. One game drops you into a room and lets you read a leader's promise, then shows you the facts the original audience never got to see — so you feel how far a message can move you before you know the whole story. One teaches teamwork by letting you notice things about a partner, then asks what you'd actually do. One is a tool that helps a group of teachers agree on how they're grading, so their scores actually mean the same thing.

They all run on a single web page, work without internet, and keep everything on your own device. A student can play one in about five minutes.
