How studios build partner networks — and why ours waits

A decode of FableVision's and Filament Games' network strategies, filed under the one finding that governs all of it: scaling waits until a real student plays a real game.

The finding that leads

Do not build a partner network yet. Both studios' networks are downstream of shipped, played, evidence-backed games. Filament's first grant came five years in, after playable prototypes with real download numbers. FableVision's "mission magnet" only pulls partners because a 30-year award-winning portfolio proves the mission first.

Tight Spiral has zero games played by students yet. Choose Your Leader is content-complete; EN195 and the capstone shipped but haven't been run with a class. Every partner door asks the same question — "what have students done with your game?" — and today the answer is "nothing yet."

The "let's scale" instinct is the optimization-before-implementation trap at the studio level. The plan below is real and worth keeping. It is filed for after the gate clears, not before.

The one move that unlocks everything below

Run one game (Choose Your Leader or EN195) with one MassBay section this fall. Capture three things: did they engage, what did they learn, what would you change. That single artifact is the key to every door in this spec. It is also Filament's literal Phase I move.

Model A — FableVision: the mission magnet

FableVision doesn't chase clients; mission-aligned institutions come to them. The pull works because the studio is itself mission-first, so it draws organizations that are also mission-based and works with them to "move the world to a better place."

The mechanics worth stealing

  • Named verticals. Partners cluster into types — publishers, broadcasters, nonprofits, museums, health. They name the cluster, then serve it.
  • A door-opener per vertical. When a partner-type recurs, they hire someone the community already knows to own it — e.g. a Director of G.L.A.M. (Galleries, Libraries, Archives, Museums) Innovation. Networks scale through named relationship-owners, not broadcast.
  • Deliberately wide contract range. From small work-for-hire to multi-year federally-funded grants — "everything in between" — so no single deal size is the whole business.
  • Walled-off business engine. A dedicated business/finance person keeps the studio solvent so creatives stay creative. (This is your Scout-wall rule, validated by a 30-year studio.)

Model B — Filament: the grant + RFP + SME engine

Filament's funding spine is non-dilutive federal grants (seven SBIR awards, the first in 2010). The structural truth they prove: nobody sustains high-quality classroom games on school sales alone — the survivors subsidize with grants and contract work, lean on app-store sales, or fold games into a larger curricular product.

The mechanics worth stealing

  • Grants over equity. SBIR funds let them test new products "with less financial worry" and without giving up ownership — but it's a real time investment, not free money.
  • Partner-as-playtester. University researchers gave iterative pedagogical input; independent teachers and experts reviewed at milestones; games got both studio-based and classroom-based usability testing with real students. The network is the validation pipeline.
  • The RFP-through-a-grant path. An institution wins a grant requiring public outreach, then issues an RFP to known game companies. You become "known" by shipping and showing impact.
  • A clear differentiator, proven with students. Every winning SBIR pitch starts there — the sentence you can't write yet, and one pilot makes writable.

Your first grant targets (research note, parked)

  • NEH — funds humanities/educational games. Choose Your Leader is a civics/rhetoric humanities game: a near-exact fit.
  • IES / ED SBIR — funds learning tools; ~10 Phase I and ~5 Phase II awards a year, conservative, wants high likelihood of classroom impact.
  • NSF SBIR — broader, funds cutting-edge education-science work.

Each needs a played-game-with-evidence to be credible. Gate applies.
