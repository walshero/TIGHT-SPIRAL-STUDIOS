Prepare · what responding actually is

Four things, then you're ready

Respond, don't edit

You're not fixing commas or rewriting sentences. You're telling the writer what their story did to a real reader. Save the proofreading for someone else's job.

Be specific — point to the page

"I liked it" tells the writer nothing. "The unheld hand in the second line — I felt the whole distance between you two right there" tells them everything. Name the actual word, image, or moment.

Read for the turn

Flash fiction usually hides its meaning until late and lands it quiet. Find where the piece changes — where it stops being about what you thought it was about — and tell the writer what you found there.

End with a real question

Not "was this good?" A genuine one: what you wondered, what you wanted more of, what choice you noticed them make. A real question is a gift — it gives the writer somewhere to go.

The lens experienced readers use: What's it about, under the surface? What's at stake? What changed? Where's the energy? What did it make you feel — and exactly where?

Read once for the feel. Then read again with a pen-hand.

Black Spots on the Crosswalk

Warm-up piece · by Matt Walsh · Tight Spiral Studios

On the day we went into the city to visit my mother, my father reached for my hand as we stepped to the edge of the curb, though I thought I was old enough that perhaps I didn't need to. That my father's hands were rough. That they held for utility and not for comfort.

"What are those black spots on the crosswalk?" I asked him. They were quarter and half-dollar sized, faceless.

"Those are kids who didn't hold their parents' hands," he said, and I gasped. I pulled my hand away, angry, and afraid, and then angry, at how quickly the world could change because of something a grownup says, how it could become a world where the punishment for small-bodied autonomy was a lightning bolt from the sky that left nothing but a black dot on white paint. Or were the children consumed from the inside? Their dreams and mistakes and fingers and skin sucked into the center of themselves so quickly that a glowing red orb like a superball hovered in space momentarily, and then dropped?

"You're a bad parker," I said to my father, and he looked at me sideways, like the dog used to.

"What did you say?" he said. He was smiling/not smiling.

"You can't park the car right at all, and you get the wrong mac and cheese."

I wasn't walking anymore. I was standing in the crosswalk even though the numbers were running out and the ambulance was getting louder.

Taking his hand then meant admitting something I wasn't quite prepared to admit. To feel something I wasn't ready for. And I wanted to scream at him for shrinking my soccer shirt in the dryer, for losing Madden on purpose, for making mom cry by herself in the laundry room, and for telling me that everything was going to be alright.

The language your comments are read on

What separates a 5 from a 2

Not about being nice or harsh — about being specific and useful to the writer.

5
Specific, generous, useful. Points to actual lines. Names what works and asks a real question about what's next. The writer knows exactly what you mean.
4
Specific and helpful, but stops short — names the moment without quite saying why it matters or what it opens.
3
Engaged but general. "I liked this part" without the part. The writer feels seen but not guided.
2
Vague or evaluative only. "Good job" / "needs work." No anchor in the text. The writer can't act on it.
1
Editing, not responding. Fixes commas, rewrites sentences, skips the story. Misses the point of the workshop.
