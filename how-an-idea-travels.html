Tight Spiral Productions

How an idea travels

Follow one idea from the back of a napkin to a machine a stranger can play. Every station is real. Every gate can stop it.

1

The spark

Matt, usually walking

An idea shows up. Half a metaphor, a mechanic, a thing a student said that stuck. It goes in the door as a sentence.

Why it matters: nothing gets built from a feature request. It gets built from a noticing.
2

The Medium Gate

Gate · fires first, every time

Before a single pixel: what medium teaches this best? A game? A card? A room? A sentence on a wall? Efficacy decides — never what's easiest to build.

Why it matters: most studios pick the medium first and bend the idea to fit. That's backwards, and you can feel it in the result.
3

The panel convenes

Five seats · silent unless one has a real objection

Medium. Art direction. Who it's for. First screen. And the Aleph seat, who asks the annoying question: is this one thing, or is it a system?

Why it matters: the panel runs before the build, not after. Catching a wrong first screen at the sketch stage costs a sentence. Catching it at launch costs the build.
4

The build

One file. Offline. No account. Nothing kept.

A single HTML file you could email to someone. It runs on a plane. It stores nothing about you. It works on a ten-year-old phone.

Why it matters: a tool that needs a login, a server, and a subscription is a tool a teacher can't actually use in March when the wifi is down.
5

Studio Eyes

Gate · a machine, not a memory

Every push, a real browser opens the file on a real phone screen — in light and in the dark. It measures contrast, tap targets, focus rings. It cannot be talked out of it.

Why it matters: I have retinitis pigmentosa. If accessibility is a thing someone has to remember, it will get forgotten on a Tuesday. So it isn't a habit here. It's a gate.
6

Cold play

Gate · the founder, on a phone, no warm-up

I pick it up cold, like a stranger would, and ask two questions: does it still do its job? did anything surprise me? Nobody else can run this gate for me.

Why it matters: the person who built it always knows where to click. That's exactly why they're the wrong person to test it — unless they can forget.
▶

A stranger plays it.

And learns a writing move by doing it — not by being told about it.

Play. Notice. Design.
Six stations. Three of them are gates that can stop the idea cold. That's the point: most things should not ship.
