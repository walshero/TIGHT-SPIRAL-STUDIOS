# English Major: The Game — Build Map

*Single source of "why this is built the way it is." Companion to `massbay-english-major.html`. Newest decisions at the bottom of each section.*

---

## The spine (one sentence)

**Teach a community college student to problem-solve their way to a degree — bend the catalog plan to a real life, and learn the real moves that protect completion while life happens.**

This is a *noticing-and-deciding* game in the studio's line: the player notices what their life demands, then makes (or fumbles) the move that keeps the degree alive. The lesson lives in the decisions, not in any lecture.

## Wireframe novelty (the one structural surprise)

The life survey rewrites the board *before* play, and a mid-degree disruption rewrites it *again*. The student watches the catalog's four-semester fiction bend into a realistic five-, six-, or seven-semester part-time path — and the game names that bending as competent planning, not failure. The novelty survives stripping all art: it's a move the architecture makes, not paint.

## Disciplinary bench (Stage 0.5 — the signature move)

This game is *about* student persistence and completion. The minds seated:

- **Tinto (departure/integration) — spine.** Students leave when academic and social integration fail. The life-meters *are* integration dimensions; each resource-reach is an integration event. This is the game's reason to exist.
- **Rendón (validation) + Goldrick-Rab (basic needs) — core mechanic.** Setbacks are basic-needs and validation events, because that's where CC students actually fall off. Reaching for help is framed as a *validating act of strength*, never deficit.
- **Gee (situated identity) — loop.** The player *is* a CC student making real moves, not reading about them.
- **Saunders + Piaget — entry.** Open in a scene; let the learner construct the lesson rather than receive it.
- **Freire — throughout.** Problem-posing, never depositing. Every teachable moment poses "what's the smart move?" and explains the why *after* the player commits — it never lectures first.

## The teachable moves (examples of the texture, not a fixed menu)

Each "decision" space offers a naive / panic / strategic choice, then explains the *why* and names *who to ask*. Built so far:

1. **The Incomplete trap.** An Incomplete is not a pause button — with no written finish plan and a missed deadline, it converts to an F. The move: take it only with a concrete completion agreement and date, or consider withdrawal.
2. **Strategic withdrawal.** A W protects the GPA where an F damages it — but it's a real decision (affects financial-aid satisfactory academic progress; there's a refund deadline). Done early and informed, dropping one course on purpose can save the semester.
3. **Drop to part-time on purpose.** Going part-time isn't falling behind; it's how ~69% of MassBay students actually finish. A realistic load you complete beats an ambitious one you don't.

The frame is extensible: any real completion move (financial-aid appeals, prerequisite sequencing, transfer-articulation timing, repeating a course for GPA) drops into the same naive/panic/strategic shape in the EDIT block.

**Fact-Check rule honored:** the game teaches the *move* and the *questions to ask*, and routes exact dates (withdrawal deadline, Incomplete conversion, refund schedule) to the advisor and Registrar — it prints **no fabricated deadline**, because those vary every term.

## Real data used (no invented statistics)

- First-to-second-semester retention **72.7%** overall (Fall 2024→Spring 2025); **80%** full-time first-time. (Fact Book §VII.)
- **~69% part-time** — grounds the whole part-time-is-normal frame. (Fact Book §II.)
- **~43%** of first-time students receive federal grant aid, avg **~$5,600**. (Fact Book §X.)
- The multi-semester pace is **modeled** as a typical part-time route — explicitly *not* cited as a completion statistic, because the Fact Book doesn't report per-program time-to-degree.

## Assessment layer (instructor-facing, hidden in play)

Each move carries the English PLO(s) it advances (the 5 verbatim from the AY2026 learning-outcomes sheet). The "Show the engine" toggle reveals them; off by default so the student's experience stays clean. This is the Outcome-Microtag pattern: evidence rides the move, not a score. (PLO → ISLO mapping is the next layer, for the Sean McCarthy / assessment conversation — not built here.)

---

## Floors held

- **Trauma-informed:** consequences land on circumstance, never the player's worth; help is always offered; wrong moves play out and recover — never a dead end. "Naming the move is the win even at the floor."
- **Accessibility / Visibility:** full-contrast ink, one decision per screen, 44px targets, keyboard nav, scroll-reset, no color-as-sole-signal, no emoji, reduced-motion respected, phone-width.
- **FERPA:** in-memory only; nothing stored, nothing transmitted; resets on close.

## Production stage

**Mechanic Prototype** (Stage 4). Allowed to look plain. The loop, the survey-reshapes-plan novelty, and the teachable-move shape are what's being proven. **Not** yet a Paper Craft work-up — that's gated behind a proven loop (your playtest).

## Spend-one-affordance (close-out)

Deliberately **flat** for this prototype — justified: the teaching moments are reading-and-deciding, and stillness chosen on purpose is allowed. A device affordance (e.g. a haptic "tick" when the plan re-shapes) is a candidate for the work-up, not the prototype.
