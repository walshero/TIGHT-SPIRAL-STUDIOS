# Choose Your Leader — The Period Bible
### Art direction for the composite room: era-dated photographic environments + withheld figures

*Tight Spiral Studios · walshero@gmail.com*
*A production spec, not a built asset. The Lumino/photographic layers described here cannot be rendered in the offline sandbox — they are produced in an image-capable session and composited into `choose-your-leader-full.html`. The figures stay non-photographic in every case (the game's spine: you judge what you were allowed to see, not the face).*

---

## The brief in one line

**The room sets the world; the broadcast lands the exact year.** Before the player reads a single word, the picture should say *which decade they are standing in* — and the screen inside that room should say *which year*. The period is not the subject. It is the air.

This is the studio's first deliberate **composite build** (OS §3.2 per-layer Medium Gate; the Compositor seat, §5, owns the seam). Two layers, two lanes, on purpose:

| Layer | Lane | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **The room** (walls, furniture, light, broadcast image, grain) | Lumino / photographic (Super Sketchy Graphics, AI-assisted, labeled) | Realism dates the era. Flat shapes cannot carry Kodachrome-vs-faded-color. This is the lane that fixes "I disengaged at paper craft." |
| **The figures** (the leader on-screen, the empty chair that is *you*) | Non-photographic — withheld silhouette / glow / blank | A real face reintroduces the bias the game strips. The person must stay a blank the player is *not yet allowed to fill in*. |

**The seam is the meaning.** A vivid, specific, dated *world* — and a deliberately blank *person* inside it. The world seduces; the figure is withheld. The Compositor's HALT: light, grain, and scale must reconcile across that seam so it reads as intention, not as an unfinished layer.

---

## The team in the room (who is directing this)

Imagine the *Mad Men* production wing, seated as named roles. Each owns one lever of period visual rhetoric:

- **Production Designer** — owns the room: furniture, set decoration, the *optimism level* of the space.
- **Director of Photography / Colorist** — owns the color science: the film stock the era was shot on, the temperature, the saturation, the fade.
- **Period-Tech Consultant** — owns the broadcast image: what a television of that exact year actually showed, in black-and-white or color, sharp or smeared.
- **Costume & Texture** — owns the materials in the room: fabric, wood, the wear of the decade.
- **The Compositor (Tight Spiral seat)** — owns the seam between the photographic room and the withheld figure; holds the HALT.

The four period levers, stated plainly so they can be directed without drawing: **color science, the broadcast image, grain/degradation, and the room's optimism level.**

## The era ladder (the through-line across three scenes)

The three Cold War scenes are **three different visual worlds**, not one room redressed. The arc of the decade is itself an argument the game makes:

- **1962 — crisp, cool, hopeful.** Kodachrome saturation, hard tailoring, the New Frontier. The dread arrives *into* an optimistic, orderly world — which is what makes it land.
- **1964 — the same world, a year heavier.** Still orderly, but the midnight hour and the war drain the warmth. Transitional.
- **1969 — soft, warm, decaying.** The optimism has gone to seed. Warmer, faded, a little unstable. The color is bleeding in at the same moment the trust is bleeding out.

The player should *feel the decade pass* across the three scenes without being told a date.

---

## Scene 1 — JFK, October 22, 1962

**The world (Production Designer + DP).** A 1962 living room photographed the way 1962 photographed itself: **Kodachrome — saturated, slightly cool, optimistic.** Hard-edged mid-century furniture, nothing worn yet, everything still believed-in. The room is *orderly and hopeful*, which is the point: the threat of nuclear war is entering a world that thought it was safe. Cool relief in the walls so the one warm thing — the screen — pulls the eye.

**The broadcast (Period-Tech Consultant).** **Black-and-white.** In October 1962, color broadcast existed but was rare — most households watched in B&W, and most networks beyond NBC had no regular color programming. The address is **crisp, high-contrast monochrome with visible scan lines**, the image a little hot in the highlights. This is historically true *and* rhetorically perfect: the most consequential message of the era arrives in austere black-and-white. The screen is the only light source in the room.

**Grain.** Fine film grain, clean. 1962 is not yet degraded — the wear comes later in the decade.

**The figure (withheld).** The President is a **faint luminous presence on the B&W screen** — the suggestion of a head and shoulders, never a resolved face. He is made of the same scan lines as the broadcast. The empty armchair in the foreground is *you*: cool, in shadow, a warm rim where the screen-light catches its edge.

**Held breath:** *"It's late. Nobody's gone to bed."* The unstable-signal flicker (already in the build) lives on the screen glow.

---

## Scene 2 — LBJ, August 4, 1964 (midnight)

**The world.** The same kind of room, **one year heavier**. Still orderly, but it's near midnight and the warmth is draining. Slightly cooler, slightly more tired than '62. The Kodachrome optimism is starting to thin. A reading chair, not a showroom chair — this room is lived in now.

**The broadcast.** **Still black-and-white** — 1964 is overwhelmingly monochrome. But the address *interrupts late programming*, so the screen reads as a hard cut-in: a grave figure, two flags, a bare wall, the image stark against the dark room. The B&W is colder than '62 — less hopeful contrast, more institutional gray.

**Grain.** A touch more present than '62. The signal feels a hair less certain (matches the content: the second attack that likely never happened).

**The figure (withheld).** A grave silhouette behind a podium-shape on the B&W screen. Still no face. The flags read as *shapes of flags*, not detailed cloth — suggest, don't simulate.

**Held breath:** *"Almost midnight. The regular program just stopped."*

---

## Scene 3 — Nixon, November 3, 1969

**The world.** **The hinge of the decade.** The room is **warmer, softer, faded** — late-60s color science, Ektachrome gone amber, the New Frontier optimism now decayed. The furniture is the same era but worn at the corners now. This is the visual rhetoric of an era curdling: warmth that reads as *tiredness*, not hope.

**The broadcast.** **The first uneasy color.** By 1969 color broadcast is spreading but far from universal and far from stable — early color sets ran warm and smeared, the image not yet locked. Nixon's address can read as **early, unstable color** — slightly oversaturated flesh tones bleeding at the edges, the picture wavering. (If a B&W reading is preferred for an older set, that ambiguity is *itself* 1969 — the year the country was split on everything, including whether it even saw the same picture.) The "direct appeal down the camera" means the figure faces the room more squarely than the others.

**Grain.** The most degraded of the three — signal noise, a warm cast, the physical wear of the late decade. Atmosphere as evidence.

**The figure (withheld).** A presence looking *straight out of the set, at the room* — but still not a resolved face. In early-color treatment, a warm smear where a face would be. The withholding is hardest here because the rhetoric is most intimate ("to you, the great silent majority") — which is exactly why the blank matters most.

**Held breath:** *"He's looking straight out of the set. Right at the room."*

## The withheld-figure rule (applies to every scene — the Compositor's law)

The photographic room can be as real and specific as the lane allows. **The figure cannot.** Across all three:

- The leader is **made of the broadcast** — scan lines in '62/'64, an unstable color smear in '69. He is never a photograph, never a likeness, never a resolved face. He is *the image the era was allowed to see*, rendered as exactly that and no more.
- The empty chair is **you** — anyone. Neutral, in shadow, catching the screen's edge-light. It is never a specific person and never occupied.
- **No real faces. Ever.** This is the same rule that keeps the figures out of the licensed-photo lane (OS §3.2): a real face hands the player the charisma and the halo, which is the precise bias the game exists to strip.

If the photographic room and the withheld figure ever stop reconciling — the room lit one way, the figure lit from nowhere; the grain on the room not carrying onto the screen image — the Compositor HALTs. The seam must read as *intention* (vivid world, withheld person), never as an unfinished layer.

## Production notes (for the capable session)

- **Lane & label.** Lumino/photographic art is AI-assisted, credited "Art commissioned to Super Sketchy Graphics by Tight Spiral Studios · AI-assisted." Not CC-BY, not for reuse.
- **No real likenesses.** Generate *rooms and broadcast textures*, not portraits. The figure is always the withheld treatment above.
- **Sourcing honesty.** If any real reference (period photo, film still) is used to guide a generation, it informs the *room*, never a person, and is attributed per the sourcing ethos.
- **Block before texture.** Standing production MO: first pass blocks composition, POV, and the light source in line; second pass adds the photographic/grain treatment. Never texture a shot that isn't blocked.
- **Composite handoff.** Each room is produced at the scene's aspect, then composited into the existing shell behind the live interactive layer (the notice-hits, the prompt, the flicker). The interactive figures/UI stay in the current non-photographic treatment.
- **Accessibility holds.** Grain and color are atmosphere, never the sole carrier of information. Any rhetoric the image carries also lives in the alt-text and the read-lines. The Visibility floor (full-contrast readable text) is untouched by the room going photographic.

## Open founder calls

1. **1969 broadcast — early color or ambiguous B&W?** Early unstable color is the stronger period statement; ambiguous B&W is the safer historical reading. (Recommend: early unstable color — it lands the year hardest, which is what you asked for.)
2. **How withheld is withheld?** Scan-line presence vs. pure glow vs. silhouette — a feel call only the founder's eyes can make.
3. **Does the room replace the cut-paper scene entirely, or sit behind a thinned cut-paper foreground?** (Recommend: photographic room, non-photographic figures/chair on top — the cleanest version of the seam.)
4. **Scope:** this graduates CYL from shipped cut-paper mechanic to a **full Lumino work-up** — a deliberate promotion, produced in a capable session, not a sandbox patch.
