How sure are you — and what would change your mind?

Science is not a pile of facts to memorize. It is a loop: hold a belief, name in advance what would prove it wrong, meet the evidence, and update — by however much the evidence earns.

Belief one · the drop test

The Drop Test

Aristotle taught it for two thousand years, and your own eyes seem to confirm it every time you drop a coin and a tissue. Say how sure you are — then name what would actually settle it.

The claim

Drop a bowling ball and a feather together, and the ball hits the ground first.

Because it is heavier. Right?

The evidence

In 1971, on the airless surface of the Moon, Apollo 15 commander David Scott held out a hammer and a falcon feather and let go. They hit the dust at the same instant. In a vacuum chamber on Earth, a bowling ball and a feather fall together too.

What slowed the feather here on Earth was never its weight. It was the air — the one variable your everyday eyes could never remove.

NASA, Apollo 15 hammer–feather drop, 1971 · Galileo, Two New Sciences, 1638.

The move that mattered

The claim wasn't wrong because Aristotle was foolish. It was wrong because nobody had removed the one thing — air — that hid the answer. Naming that test, in advance, is the science. The result is just what falls out of it.

Belief two · the 10% brain

The 10% Brain

It's in movies, ads, and self-help books: unlock the other 90% and you'd be a genius. It feels true — we all know we could focus better. But feeling true and being true are different tests.

The claim

We only use 10% of our brains. The other 90% sits idle.

Imagine what you'd do with the rest.

Right now, how likely is the claim TRUE?

50%
certainly falsecertainly true

Before any evidence — what would change your mind?

The evidence

PET and fMRI scans show activity spread across the whole brain, even during rest and sleep. There is no silent 90%: damage to almost any region — a stroke the size of a dime — produces a real, specific deficit. If 90% were spare, most strokes would do nothing.

The brain is about 2% of body weight and burns about 20% of your energy. Evolution does not pay that bill to keep nine-tenths of an organ switched off. No one has ever found the source of the "10%" figure.

Neuroimaging & lesion literature; brain metabolic-rate estimates (~20% of resting energy).

The move that mattered

A claim can feel true, sell books, and appear everywhere — and still fail the first real test. "Everyone says so" is not evidence. The question that breaks the myth is small and answerable: if 90% were idle, what would a brain scan look like?

Belief three · the full moon

The Full Moon

Nurses swear by it. Police swear by it. The word lunatic is built on it. When a belief is this widely shared by experienced people, it feels settled — which is exactly when the scientific move is most useful.

The claim

A full moon makes people act strangely — more ER visits, more crime, more chaos.

Everybody who works nights knows it.

Right now, how likely is the claim TRUE?

55%
certainly falsecertainly true

Before any evidence — what would change your mind?

The evidence

Researchers have done exactly that count, many times. A review pooling dozens of studies found no reliable link between the lunar phase and ER visits, crime, psychiatric admissions, or births. The quiet full-moon nights happen just as often — they just don't get remembered.

The belief survives on confirmation bias: a wild night under a full moon gets filed as proof; an identical night under a half moon gets forgotten. Counting beats remembering.

Rotton & Kelly, "Much ado about the full moon," Psychological Bulletin 97 (1985), and later reviews.

The loop that is science

  • Hypothesis — state the belief so it could be proven wrong. "Heavier falls faster" is science; "some things are meant to fall" is not — nothing could test it.
  • Evidence — go to the world and weigh what it actually shows. A story, a feeling, or a crowd agreeing are not the same as a count in a vacuum.
  • Revision — move your confidence by however much the evidence earns. Not all the way for a rumor; a long way for a clean test. Then run the loop again.
