## Why a series, not a monograph

The work spans several systems — a recursive design method, a review-panel architecture, an assessment pipeline, and shipped games. Presented all at once, they break coherence for any reader without context (the founder's own hardest lesson: *do not present too many systems at once*). A monograph forces the whole river to be held at once, for years. A series solves this structurally: **each paper carries ONE system, makes ONE claim, shows ONE exhibit, and cross-references the others** ("for the assessment architecture, see Paper III"). This mirrors the studio's own ring model — a lead paper at the center, companions radiating outward — and its recursive method: ship one, learn, tighten, ship the next.

**On credentials.** This is practitioner scholarship — a respected lane that does not require a PhD. The warrant is twenty years of portfolio-assessment work, three years of Pedagogy-of-Real-Talk PD, the COREC build, AI Task Force membership, and a working studio with shipped, accessible artifacts. The author's note carries this; no doctorate is needed to publish in the venues below.

## The four papers

### Paper I — The Lead (write this one first)
**Working title:** *The Humanist's Pipeline: How a Writing Specialist Built a Working Educational-Technology System Without Writing Code*

**The claim.** A learning specialist with no coding background built a functioning production system — multiple accessible educational games, an assessment on-ramp, a review-panel governance model — in a matter of days, by applying *assessment and pedagogy training* rather than engineering. The constraint (no code, degenerative vision, a humanist's lens) was the design advantage, not the deficit.

**What it does.** Frames the whole series. Tells the practitioner narrative: the assessment-review-day friction that started it ("we can't push five outcomes to a ten-year cycle"), the decision to build, the ten-day arc. Points to the other three papers; embeds nothing heavy. This is the Chronicle-of-Higher-Ed-adjacent piece and the first thing a non-specialist reader (e.g. a spouse, a dean) can read and understand.

**Feeds from:** `what-this-is.html` (the plain-language translation, three audiences — this is nearly Paper I's spine already), the autobiography threads from the 2026-06-28 conversation (Boston Latin Socratic method; MetroWest Tech-Prep nursing test-prep; Evergreen learning-communities; Japan + compound metaphors; AWA / Deb Bloomberg / Peter Elbow; MIT 2012; COREC with Katie; 20 years of portfolio assessment).

**Length:** 4,000–6,000 words. **Status:** the only paper that must be written fresh; the others largely exist.

### Paper II — The Method
**Working title:** *Recursive Design as Editorial Practice: A Named Review Panel for Human-in-the-Loop Production*

**The claim.** A system that improves by recurrence (play → notice → refine → tighten), governed by a cast of named review roles each guarding one failure mode, with hard human gates (HALT/BAIL/SCOPE), produces better process *and* product than freeform LLM use — because adding requires pruning and nothing graduates to a rule unless a friction recurs.

**Feeds from:** `the-recursive-loop.html` (the loop, already playable and self-documenting), `tight-spiral-studio-os__6_.md` §5 (the panel roster), §6 (the pipeline + gate outcomes), §6.2.5 (the recursive OS-improvement loop). Largely written — this is assembly + framing, not invention.

**Length:** 5,000–7,000 words. **Venue lean:** *Journal of Interactive Technology and Pedagogy*, *Hybrid Pedagogy*.

### Paper III — The Assessment Architecture (exhibit-heavy)
**Working title:** *From Artifact to Institution: An On-Ramp/Trunk Model for Assignment Alignment and Rater Norming*

**The claim.** Assessment can be made transparent and faculty-owned — not rented from a vendor — through a two-part architecture: an **Auditor** (on-ramp) that aligns an assignment to a rubric before any student work is scored, and **Confluence** (trunk) that norms raters so two readers agree. Evidence rides the seven rings (artifact → assignment → course → department → program → institution → the outer free-thinking gear) and rolls up to the ISLO layer.

**Feeds from:** `assignment-auditor.html` (BUILT this session — the on-ramp, scores AAC&U Written Communication, runs the OS visibly, hands off to Confluence), `confluence-TRUNK-2026-06-23.html`, the MassBay placement portfolio rubric (1–6) and 7 ISLOs/PLOs, `decision-auditor-onramp.md`. This is the paper where live exhibits embed as base64 iframes (confirmed ~124KB, fits one HTML file).

**Length:** 6,000–8,000 words. **Venue lean:** *Assessment Update*, *To Improve the Academy*, *Journal of Writing Assessment*.

### Paper IV — The Worked Example
**Working title:** *Choosing the Leader You Were Allowed to See: A Trauma-Informed Media-Literacy Game and Its Design*

**The claim.** A five-minute accessible game can teach a transferable critical-literacy reflex — noticing the gap between what was said and what was true — without lecturing, by making the player commit before the record turns, and can carry real emotional stakes safely through three trauma-informed rails.

**Feeds from:** `choose-your-leader-full.html` (BUILT, content-complete), `choose-your-leader-map.md`, `cyl-period-bible.md`, `cyl-sound-period-bible.md`, the disciplinary bench (Freire, Saunders, Squire, Gee, Hall, McLuhan, Postman, Arendt). Nearly fully documented already.

**Length:** 5,000–7,000 words. **Venue lean:** *Games and Culture*, *Journal of Games, Self, & Society*, *Well Played*.
