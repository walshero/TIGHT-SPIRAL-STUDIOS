Who holds the room?

One ordinary campus scene. No villains. You'll read it three ways — the same way the college's own DEI rubric does: who holds power in it, how the same moment lands differently on different people, and what it looks like to actually say something.

This isn't a quiz with a right answer to catch you out. A starting point is never a verdict. You're measured against the rubric's levels and your own growth — never against anyone else.

The scene · it does not change

A required course. Week 10. The professor announces a group project, assigns the groups, and says: “You'll need to meet outside class to finish it. Figure out a time that works and get it done.”

Four students land in one group. Each one wants to do well. Each one hears the same sentence.

Read one · systems of power

Who does this plan quietly build for?

“Figure out a time that works” sounds neutral. But every neutral-sounding rule is built around a default person — the one it assumes. Whoever fits the default holds the power in the room without anyone deciding they should. Select every assumption baked into this plan.

The structure

Every one of those is an assumption the plan makes — . None of them is written down. That's what makes them structural: the rules were set by someone with the institutional power to set them (the professor, and behind them the college's norms), and they quietly assume a default student — one with a car, open time, no caregiving, easy English, and the confidence to run a room.

Whoever matches that default holds power in this group without doing anything. Whoever doesn't will spend energy just getting to the starting line that the default student never spends. That gap wasn't chosen by anyone in the room. It was built into the design.

Where are you in it?

Nothing here is recorded. Just notice: which parts of that default fit your life, and which don't? The rubric's top level isn't seeing the structure — it's seeing your own place inside it.

Read two · group & individual interactions

One sentence. Four different landings.

The professor's sentence was identical for everyone: “meet outside class and figure it out.” Watch it land on each person in the group. Open all four — each is a whole person doing their best, not a problem to be solved.

She's up at 5, closes the store at 11. She hears “figure out a time” and already knows every time the group picks will be a time she's on the clock — and that asking them to work around her shift will make her sound like the difficult one. She's not less committed. She's carrying more, and the plan makes her carry it alone.

Evenings are pickup, dinner, homework — not his, theirs. A “quick Saturday meet” means finding and paying for childcare, or bringing a four-year-old to a study room. He'll do the work at midnight after the house is asleep. The plan assumes his time is his to give. Most of it already belongs to someone smaller.

On paper she's one of the sharpest in the class. But “figure it out” happens fast, out loud, in overlapping talk — the one setting where her second language costs her most. By the time she's shaped the sentence, the group has moved on and quietly filed her as quiet. The plan rewards the loudest scheduler, not the strongest thinker.

For Tyler, “figure out a time” genuinely is easy — his calendar is open, the library is a five-minute walk, and speaking up in a group has never once cost him anything. None of that is his fault, and none of it makes him a bad teammate. It just means the plan fits him like a glove and he may never notice it was cut to his measurements.

How it lands

One instruction, four different costs — and they line up almost perfectly with who has power outside the room. That's the thing to see: the plan didn't create the inequality, but it passed it straight through, unchanged, and called the result “fair because everyone got the same rule.”

Individual: three of four spend energy just reaching the start. Community: the group loses Aigerim's thinking and reads Marisol as difficult. Society: repeat this design across a whole degree and it quietly decides who finishes college — not by talent, by fit.

Read three · advocacy

You're in the group. You can speak.

You've seen the structure and felt the landings. Now the group is picking a time and looking at their phones. What do you actually do? There's no gotcha — pick the one closest to what you'd really say, then see what it does and where the college's rubric places it. You can try another.

Why advocacy is a skill, not a personality

Notice the ladder isn't about being loud or nice. It climbs from staying silent, to naming a problem, to offering a strategy, to changing the design and owning your place in it. Every rung is learnable. Wherever you are today is a starting point, not a verdict.

The instrument you were just read on

What actually fixes the room

  • Deliberative interdependence — design the work so people genuinely rely on each other, with clear rules for how it gets done, so “community” is built into the mechanism instead of left to whoever has time.
  • Transformative translation — let people bring the work through their own experience and more than one way in, so the strongest thinker isn't lost to the fastest talker.
  • Proactive engagement — move the support to the center: build feedback and flexible timing into the design, instead of waiting for the person carrying the most to raise their hand and ask.
