Flash Compression · Intake + Capture

The Flash Compression Game

Stage −1 intake + session capture, 2026-06-29. A remember, not the game. Carry this out.

The lock that governs everything below: this is not gamification. Learning principles drive the instructional design. The intake drives the build, the build drills down, and the rhetorical situation drives the art — every time. The human (you) stays the principal at every gate.

The spine

A student learns compression by playing the moves on a neutral draft you wrote — not their own work, not AI-generated. They play a card, the move happens on the page, and the naming comes after (the meta-tag recognizes what they just did). Learn by playing. Confluence sits underneath as the assessment layer.

The cards — your taxonomy (set live this session)

Two kinds of card, marked differently. Some moves fire once per piece; others stack.

First Domino SINGULAR
Yours · only one per piece
The first thing that tips, that makes everything after it fall. One per story.
Start After the Storm SINGULAR
Bought in · only one per piece
Begin when everything but the action is over. Your theater principle: something big just happened offstage. The reader walks in mid-consequence.
World Detail That Matters PLURAL
Yours · many per piece · title kept plain for now (better metaphor later)
A concrete detail that does real work — not a token or totem, just a detail that carries weight. You can have many. Order them, let them lock the world in.
Interior Weather PLURAL
Yours · many per piece
The felt-state made visible — the image holds the feeling so you never state it.
Backstory Snap PLURAL
Yours · many per piece
History implied in a flash, not delivered. The reader infers the weight; you don't explain it.
Slide PLURAL
Yours · many per piece · a transition that carries weight
A shift — in status, distance, or relation — that moves the piece without announcing the move. (You flagged: the right example isn't a literal prop hand-off; it's the quieter relational shift. Example TBD in your voice.)

The method, the way you set it

Start small. Get the right thing good and small first, then magnify. Build up from crap and you get crap. So: lock the cards before mechanics. The order is non-negotiable.

Saunders — entry/compression. Compression is a pressure, not a topic. Pick the moves that put the screw on; don't add a card because it's clever.
Le Guin — constraint as revelation. Name the moves as gifts, not rules. A card is permission, not a red pen.
Cognitive Load theorist. What "Interior Weather" actually asks of the brain — the minimum signal so a move lands without overload.
Learning Scientist (Gee, semiotic domains). The moves must be discipline-recognized — a real flash writer would call this work by its name.
Affordance Director. The one device capability the game spends for real surprise — harness what makes people linger, for good.
Variable-Reward Honesty. Guards the dark-pattern line: teaching anticipation vs. manufacturing compulsion. HALT.
Peak-End. One deliberate peak; an ending that doesn't trail off.

The recursive pipeline (the shape to document)

Stage −1 · Intake (you, the human gate)
Name what it teaches (compression via the cards), what students play on (neutral draft), who's seated. This file is that intake.
Stage 0 · Lock the cards
Filter every candidate move through you. Singular vs. plural marked. Drafty Air harvested and filtered. Final deck locked BEFORE mechanics.
Stage 0.5 · Disciplinary bench
Saunders, Le Guin, you-as-writer. Each card audited against the craft it claims.
Stage 1 · Neutral draft IS the prototype
You write the draft. The moves prove themselves in real play before any UI is built.
Stages 2–3 · Parallel tracks
Track A: learning-science validation (does the move land? load hold?). Track B: engagement (one device moment, honest reward, the peak). They write each other's requirements.
Stage 4 · Convergence
Both tracks report back; the affordance becomes functionally necessary for learning, not decorative.
Stage 5 · Ship draft + interface together
Co-designed. Fun and learning are the same gesture.

The payoff (what hasn't been done before)

Most design splits learning (curriculum people) from engagement (UX people) and hopes they meet at the end. This co-authors them from the start: the neutral draft proves the learning, the interface proves the engagement for that learning. A student can't separate "this is fun" from "this is how I learned the move" — because they're the same thing.
