Your course is a river.

It runs one direction, May 26 to July 3, and every workshop is a bend. Text: Sellers, The Practice of Creative Writing, 4th ed. Plan roughly 20 hours a week — six weeks doing fifteen weeks' work.

Four workshops. Tap one to see what it asks.

Each workshop is the same shape: you make a thing, you revise one for the group, you read your group as a writer would.

The move: take something real and give it form without lying about it.

  • Draft two: a Secret World and a Moving Images piece (750–1500 words each).
  • Revise one for workshop.
  • Read your group: two margin comments + a 100–200 word end note per piece.

The move: compression and image, with the training wheels of rhyme deliberately off.

  • Write a Where I'm From poem (150–300 words) and a free-verse poem (no rhyming — required).
  • Revise one for workshop.
  • Respond to your group's poems following the peer-response guide.

The move: a happy protagonist with every desire met is boring. Build tension.

  • Write a 10-minute play (1,000–1,500 words). Read it aloud, time it: 9–11 minutes.
  • Include original title, cast of characters, setting, and at-rise.
  • Respond to your group: five margin comments + a 100–200 word summary per play.

The move: a complete story that earns its brevity — no chapter one, no "to be continued."

  • Write a flash story (750–2000 words), any genre, fully self-contained.
  • Final workshop reads differently: you take a reader role on each piece.
  • Practice the three roles below before you comment — that's the arcade.

The practice arcade · optional · extra credit

Three reader roles. Practice the move, then read for it.

In Workshop 4 you take a different reader role on each piece. Rehearse each on a short snippet. Comments on gameplay are worth extra credit — see the schedule.

Find the line doing the work of three.

Read the opening. One line is the real first sentence; the rest is wind-up. Which line could the story start on?

It was a cold morning, the kind of morning that feels colder than it is. She had woken early. She got up and made coffee and stood at the window. The moving truck was already in the driveway next door, and the man unloading it was wearing her dead husband's coat.

Spot the abstraction with no picture under it.

Three of these put something in front of your eyes. One names a feeling instead of showing it. Which one blurs?

Which ending lands sideways on an object or gesture?

A good flash ending turns — it doesn't just halt. One closes on a concrete gesture that carries the whole story. Which one turns?

The compression climb · the workshop-4 revision move, made physical

Sandbags: cut the dead weight, watch it fly.

A flash piece is a balloon carrying sandbags. Every word that doesn't lift is ballast. Tap a line to cut it. When only the lifting words are left, the piece leaves the ground.

Where the river ends: the portfolio.

No final exam. In Week 6 you gather your revised pieces and write an artist statement, then submit the portfolio in the Start Here section of Blackboard. Comments and your final grade arrive by email within a week or so of the last day.

That's the whole course. Make things, read like a writer, revise, gather. Congratulations in advance.
