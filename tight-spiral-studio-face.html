      Four doors — pick where you're standing
      The Rube Goldberg gate-spine

      One chain. Each stage knocks the next loose only when its gate passes. Pick a build, set where it sits, and the spine shows what's done, where you are, and the next gate to fire. This is the moat made visible — the machinery that keeps a build honest before it ships.

        Playtester — the two-ledger loop

        Play the build once. Hold exactly two questions while you do:

        • Ledger A — Fidelity (binary, unforgiving): does it still do the job it was built for? A miss is a HALT, not a note.
        • Ledger B — Emergence (captured, never scored): did anything surprise me? Write it down; it goes to the parking lot, it does not change the build mid-play.

        The Drift Fork: when a surprise shows up, ask one thing — does this serve the objective, or is it auditioning to replace it? Serves, fold it in. Replaces, park it by name. Solo builders don't drift from bad ideas; they drift from exciting ones. The fork catches the exciting ones.

        Client — intake and the trade

        Every "build this now" names what it displaces. Attention is the scarcest resource here; nothing gets added without something being moved, parked, or cut. The intake asks three things:

        • What do you want from this idea? Build now, park, delegate, or cut.
        • What does building it now displace? Name the thing that waits.
        • Which layer does it serve — the calibration spine, or the game costume? When they conflict, the spine wins.

        The full intake form is a separate instrument; this door is the posture, not the paperwork.

        Player — the other side of the screen

        What the studio promises the person who actually plays:

        • Joy first. Every build opens on the thing that makes you lean in, not on a settings wall.
        • One decision per screen. Nothing hidden, nothing crowded, large type, your comfort control in the corner.
        • Nothing stored. The game keeps no record of you. If there's something to carry out, you choose to copy it — it never leaves on its own.
        • You're whole. The person is treated as capable and the author of their own work. Consequence lands on the circumstance, never on your worth.
