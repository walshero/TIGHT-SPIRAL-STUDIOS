    Why Assessment

    Why we do this at all

    Before the rubrics and the rounds of norming, the plain version. Assessment is, in Linda Suskie's words, deciding what we want students to learn and making sure they learn it. Everything else in this hub is in service of that one sentence.

    If you have ever sat through an assessment meeting wondering what it was for, you are in good company. The honest answer is that most of the work goes to gathering evidence, and the part that actually matters — going back and changing how we teach — is the part that most often never happens.

    6%

    When Trudy Banta and colleagues reviewed 146 institutional accounts of good assessment practice, only about six percent showed evidence that student learning had actually improved — no matter what was measured. The loop almost never closes. Confluence is built to close it.

    So this hub follows the water. A program outcome starts as a hope at the source. It runs through the courses that carry it. It reaches a real student through the work they make. And then — the stage that matters most — it runs back upstream to change what we do. Four scholars mark the four stages.

    1 Source · Program Outcome The fit between hope and proof
    Peggy Maki — assessment as the fit between what we expect and what students show

    Every program begins with a claim about what its students should be able to do. Maki calls assessment the work of checking the fit between that expectation and what students actually demonstrate. The outcome at the source is not a rule handed down — it is a hypothesis we agree to test.

    2 Pools · The Courses Evidence good enough to act on
    Linda Suskie — evidence whose quality is good enough to inform decisions

    The outcome pools in the courses that carry it. Here we gather student work — not to grade it again, but to read it together. Suskie's standard is simple and demanding: the evidence has to be good enough that we can actually use it to decide something. Weak evidence ends the conversation; usable evidence starts one.

    3 Mouth · The Student A real person, not a comparison
    Montenegro & Jankowski — equity-minded assessment centers the student, not the benchmark

    The water reaches a student through the artifact they made. Montenegro and Jankowski warn that assessment can quietly harm the students it claims to serve — most often by treating one group as the benchmark everyone else is measured against. The artifact at the mouth is one person's real work, read on its own terms.

    4 Upstream · The Return Closing the loop, or it was wasted
    Trudy Banta — if findings never change teaching, the loop never closed

    This is the stage the 6% never reach. Banta's whole argument is that assessment only counts when the findings travel back upstream and change something — a course, an assignment, the way we teach a hard idea. Everything before this is preparation. The return is the point.

    Suskie, L. (2018). Assessing Student Learning: A Common Sense Guide (3rd ed.). Jossey-Bass / Wiley. — The plain-language definition of assessment and the standard that evidence be good enough to inform decisions.
    Banta, T. W., & Blaich, C. (2011). Closing the assessment loop. Change: The Magazine of Higher Learning, 43(1), 22–27. — The finding that roughly 6% of 146 good-practice profiles showed improved learning, and the case that closing the loop is the work.
    Maki, P. L. (2004). Assessing for Learning: Building a Sustainable Commitment Across the Institution. Stylus. — Assessment as determining the fit between expectations and demonstrated learning.
    Montenegro, E., & Jankowski, N. A. (2020). A New Decade for Assessment: Embedding Equity into Assessment Praxis (Occasional Paper No. 42). National Institute for Learning Outcomes Assessment (NILOA). — Equity-minded assessment; the caution against norming one student group as the benchmark.
    These four assessment-methods voices complement the pedagogy and writing scholars credited in the Scholars panel. The work is real; the connections to design choices in this hub are documented, not generated.

    What We're Looking For — In Plain Language

    PLO 1 · Reading

    You read carefully and critically

    You don't just absorb — you question, connect, and figure out what a text is actually saying and not saying.

    PLO 2 · Writing & Voice

    You write with your own thinking

    Your writing sounds like you. You have a point of view, and you know who you're writing for and why.

    PLO 3 · Argument

    You build an argument from evidence

    You back up positions with real sources, evaluate whether they're credible, and use them to build a case.

    PLO 4 · Process

    You revise and collaborate

    Writing isn't a one-shot deal. You draft, get feedback, revise, and take the process seriously.

    PLO 5 · Language & Identity

    You think about what language does

    Language shapes who we think we are and how we relate to each other. You can see and analyze that — in your writing and in the world.

    The Watershed Year — an annotated landscape

    Five program outcomes, one five-year turn — marked like a map. Each year, one outcome runs at full depth while the other four keep flowing as sampled threads. Open gates are opportunities the design creates; shoals are the real snags, named honestly, so anyone can read the terrain and route around it. One channel never rotates out: Written Communication, read every year in EN102.

    Open gate · opportunity Shoal · barrier Return eddy · intervene Spine · Written Comm / EN102

    Open gates — what the terrain makes possible

    • O1A permanent measure point. Written Communication is read every year in EN102 — the one channel that never rotates out, so the trend line is unbroken. [EN102 · ISLO spine]
    • O2Early warning from overlap. Because all five outcomes are sampled across all courses every year, a cross-cutting problem surfaces before its focus year arrives. [AY24-25 Annual Report · rotate annually / sampled]
    • O3Findings become fixes. The eddy loops a result back the next year as an intervention, then re-checks — measurement and change overlap. [dept practice · reflect + intervene]

    Shoals — where the current catches

    • B1Reality can outrun the plan. When GenAI-influenced writing appeared, the department changed the assessment focus mid-cycle. The wheel is not rigid — a strain as well as a strength. [AY24-25 Annual Report · GenAI course-change]
    • B2The under-ready handoff. Students can move into EN102 without having demonstrated key skills — the spine reads a gap the cycle has to route around. [1/29/24 Dept Minutes · EN102 readiness]
    • B3Reporting lag. A year's finding may not be written up when the next year begins — the SLO report can be "forthcoming" while the next cycle already runs. [AY24-25 Annual Report · forthcoming SLO report]

    For Assessment Coordinators

    Running an Annual SLO Assessment

    A step-by-step guide to conducting your program's annual assessment of student learning and producing the report MassBay asks for each year.

    Which version of this guide do you want?

    The whole assessment in one sentence: each year you look back at what you changed last time and whether it worked, pick an outcome to look at this year, gather and score real student work against it, name what you found, and decide what to do next — then write it down in a short report. Every program runs this same loop; only the form and the deadline differ by institution. At MassBay this is the Assessment of Learning by Program: Annual Summary and Analysis, and these annual reports become a reviewed component of your program's five-year Academic Program Review.
    Before you start — one privacy rule that does not bend. De-identified student writing sent to any outside AI service is a HALT until your privacy basis and disclosure are confirmed, even when the text looks anonymous. Scoring and norming inside this app stay on your device. Do not paste student artifact text into an external AI tool to "help score" without that confirmation.
    CYCLE Declare this year's assessment cycle

    Before the steps below, name the cycle you're running. This is the spine every later report hangs off: which outcome, which courses, which semester, who owns it, and when it's due. Saved on this device only — nothing is fetched or uploaded.

    Declared cycles (this device)

    The steps

    Assessment is a loop, not a fresh start. Before anything else, answer three questions about last year:

    • Which outcome did your program assess last time?
    • What change did you decide to make, and which faculty were involved?
    • Did the change actually happen, and did it work?

    This is the part programs most often skip — and the part that turns assessment from paperwork into improvement. Be honest when a planned change didn't get implemented; naming that gap is itself a finding.

    On the form: this is the "Reflection on Previous Year's Assessment" section — the first block of the report.

    In this app: open the program's record and read last year's logged priorities. The app does not yet hold a place to write this reflection — that is one of the gaps this guide surfaces (see "What this guide reveals," below).

    Pick the program learning outcome (PLO) you will look at this year. Most programs rotate through their outcomes on a multi-year schedule so each one gets attention across a review cycle.

    On the form: the "Current Year's Assessment" section asks which PLO(s) you're assessing, and the multi-year grid at the end of the report shows which outcome falls in which academic year.

    In this app: the Norming and Assessment sections are organized by program outcome, so the outcome you pick here is the one your readers will score against.

    Tip: a focused question makes assessment doable. The English AY24-25 report didn't just assess "drafting and revision" in the abstract — it asked a specific live question: how is GenAI affecting students' ability to meet that outcome? A sharp question gives the whole year direction.

    Collect artifacts — the actual student work that shows whether the outcome is being met — and score them against your rubric. For reliable scoring, faculty need to norm first: read shared samples together, calibrate, and agree on what each score means before scoring independently.

    In this app: this is the heart of Confluence. Use Submit to bring artifacts in, then Norming to calibrate and score. The lens chooser, the calibration anchor, and the auto-flag when a score diverges by 2 or more all exist to keep readers calibrated — which is exactly the "frequent check-ins, challenges, and recalibrations" the English report called essential.

    Privacy: scoring happens on your device. The HALT above applies to any plan to route artifact text through an outside AI.

    A real-world note from the English department's experience: keep readers focused on the order of concern you're actually assessing. When the task is a higher-order outcome (argument, rhetorical choices), comments drift toward lower-order concerns (grammar, format). Name the tier of concern out loud before scoring.

    Turn the scores into findings. Not "we scored 47 portfolios" — but what the scoring told you about student learning. Quantitative results (how many met the outcome) and qualitative patterns (what readers noticed) both count.

    On the form: the "significant findings" section. The English report is a good model — it reported survey percentages, exit-exam observations, and reflections from a tool pilot, each as a distinct finding.

    In this app: the calibration readout and your scored data give you the raw material. Writing the findings narrative itself is currently done outside the app.

    From the findings, name your priorities, then list the concrete activities that will address them — with the faculty responsible. This is what makes next year's "close the loop" step possible: this year's plan is next year's reflection.

    On the form: the last two sections — "What priorities emerge?" and "What activities are planned?" — plus the multi-year assessment schedule grid.

    Keep the plan small enough to actually do. One or two real changes beat ten aspirational ones. An unimplemented plan becomes an awkward reflection next year.

    At MassBay: fill in the Assessment of Learning by Program form with your header (program, academic year, faculty who prepared it) and the five sections above, then submit it through your division. VERIFY the exact submission destination and annual deadline for your program with your dean or the Provost's Office — these are not specified on the form itself.

    Generally: record the loop in whatever annual form your institution requires, keep a copy your program controls, and make sure next year's coordinator can find it.

    In this app: export to your institution's report format is a planned build. Today the app exports structured JSON; it does not yet produce the MassBay narrative form.

    Where this feeds: the five-year Program Review

    Where this feeds: the longer review cycle

    Your annual SLO reports don't stand alone. MassBay's Academic Program Review runs every five years over three-to-four semesters, and the review committee explicitly reviews the program's annual assessments as part of the self-study. A program that has kept honest annual reports walks into program review with five years of evidence already in hand. The annual loop is the small spiral; program review is the wide one.
    Annual assessment is the small loop inside a larger one. Most institutions also run a periodic, multi-year program review that draws on the annual reports as evidence. Keeping good annual records is what makes the big review tractable.

    SHARE Contribute a syllabus or assignment prompt

    An optional way to build a shared, department-wide library of instructor materials — syllabi and assignment prompts. This is the one place in Confluence that can publish beyond your device, and only for instructor-authored materials. The offline core is untouched; this is an opt-in publish step.

    FERPA Instructor materials only. Syllabi and assignment prompts are instructor IP, not student records. Do not paste student writing, names, grades, or any FERPA-protected content. This form checks for name+grade patterns and student-artifact language and will stop you. Student assessment data never uses this seam.
    Not yet live. This seam is wired but dormant until the department's shared backend is provisioned. When ready, one config block below is filled once (project URL + public anon key), and row-level security — set in the backend, not in this file — is the only thing that governs who can read and write. Until then, submissions are validated and held locally so the workflow can be tested end-to-end without sending anything.

    Scholars

    Ten scholars whose ideas inform specific design choices in Confluence. Each card names the concept and shows where it lives in the hub.

    These are not AI-generated profiles. Each entry identifies how a scholar's published work shaped a decision in this hub — amplified feedback loops, zones of proximal development, situated learning, trauma-informed framing, process pedagogy. The work is real. The connections are documented.
    Paulo Freire
    Critical Pedagogue · 1921–1997
    Dialogical Education
    Pedagogy of the Oppressed (1968). The Confluence framing of norming as “where readers converge” rather than “the anchor score handed down by authority” is Freirean. The before-reflection field — asking readers to commit to a reading before seeing others’ scores — prevents the norming anchor from functioning as the teacher who already knows.
    Peter Elbow
    Writing Process Theorist · 1935–
    Risk-Taking Grading
    Embracing Contraries (1986). The EN195 grading model in the Materials panel is Elbow’s: a B guaranteed for sustained engagement and meeting specs; an A for demonstrated excellence. This decouples creative risk from grade risk.
    Mike Rose
    Community College Advocate · 1944–2015
    Against Deficit Framing
    Lives on the Boundary (1989). The plain-language PLO descriptions in the Student View panel resist the institutional voice Rose documented as exclusionary. Draft in Progress — showing work at the draft stage as evidence of learning, not failure — is directly Rosean.
    Asao B. Inoue
    Antiracist Assessment Scholar · 1967–
    Labor-Based Grading
    Antiracist Writing Assessment Ecologies (2015). The sub-component marking in the Norming panel surfaces the specific judgments inside a holistic score, making visible the rubric’s assumptions. The rescore field documents the negotiation, not just the outcome.
    bell hooks
    Engaged Pedagogy Theorist · 1952–2021
    Whole-Person Learning
    Teaching to Transgress (1994). The student bios section — placing JayJay Conrad, Diego Rocha, Lena Sebugwawo, and David Earl’s voices before any institutional data — honors the whole person and their life beyond the classroom.
    James Paul Gee
    Learning and Literacy Theorist · 1948–
    Situated Learning / Amplified Feedback
    What Video Games Have to Teach Us (2003). Walsh’s 2012 sabbatical at the MIT Learning Games Network was shaped by Gee’s work. The norming gameplay mechanics — streak counter, calibration badge, confluence reveal — are direct applications: feedback arrives at the right moment, is specific, and is cumulative.
    Janice Carello
    Trauma-Informed Pedagogy Scholar
    Seven Principles
    Trauma-Informed Pedagogies (2022). The Confluence Project Steward was renamed from “The Enforcer” on June 19, 2026 — a direct application of Carello’s trauma-informed framing. The voluntary submission flow applies her safety and trustworthiness principles.
    Pat Schneider
    Writer and Pedagogue · 1934–2020
    AWA Method / Voice
    Writing Alone and With Others (2003). Schneider’s Amherst Writers and Artists method — that every person has a strong, unique voice worth putting into the world — is the theoretical ground for the Student View panel’s plain-language PLOs. The voluntary submission flow protects the fragile new writer.
    Jared Cooney Horvath
    Neuroscientist and Educator
    Spaced Retrieval / Worked Examples
    Stop Talking, Start Influencing (2019). The norming sequence applies Horvath’s spaced retrieval and worked example principles: you score first (retrieval), see the worked example (confluence anchor), then rescore (encoding with feedback). The calibration badge applies his research on motivation.
    David H. Rose
    Founder, CAST · Universal Design for Learning
    Multiple Means
    Universal Design for Learning (1990s–). UDL’s principle that access is designed in from the start, not retrofitted, is the ground for treating accessibility as the design rather than a feature. The choose-your-instrument lens system — Rings, Petal, Bead, Ripple — is multiple means of representation: each rater norms in the visual language that reaches them. The not-color-alone rule, 44px targets, high-contrast override, and reduced-motion paths are direct UDL applications.

    Why It Looks This Way — The Decision Log

    Every choice below is a tributary that fed the channel. Each starts with the principle, then the design that followed. Open a decision to see the reasoning and its grounding.

    Five tributaries, one channel. The channel is the app you are using.

    • The Overview rings encode level by nesting, size, and a distinct texture per ring — not by hue alone. Color stays expressive, but a reader could turn the screen grayscale and still tell the rings apart.

      Grounding: Bertin's visual variables and the Cleveland–McGill / Mackinlay ranking — hue is weak for ordered or nested meaning and strong only for categories; position, size, and containment carry hierarchy. Texture is a validated redundant channel that survives color-vision deficiency and low vision (WCAG 1.4.1, use of color).

    • Xenos, Tron, and Luminocity let a program choose how it wants to see its own assessment. The choice is a first-class feature because the way a tool meets you shapes whether you engage with it.

      Grounding: Gee on identity and multiple entry points; Paul Hernandez on culturally responsive, "real-talk" pedagogy — meaning connects to who the learner already is; Csikszentmihalyi and Chen on flow — the environment fits the user's state rather than the reverse.

    • Rings, petals, beads, or ripples — the same scoring work, four ways to see it land. Preview-on-tap lets a new rater rehearse with no stakes before any score is recorded.

      Grounding: dual-coding and multiple-representation research — a concept held in more than one representation is more robustly understood; rehearsal without consequence lowers the affective filter for novices learning a new instrument.

    • High-contrast controls in fixed, predictable places; keyboard and voice operable; reduce-motion, larger text, and colorblind-safe cues one action away. High-contrast overrides every skin so it can never be out-styled.

      Grounding: Universal Design for Learning (Meyer, Rose & Gordon, 2014) — design for the edges and the center benefits too. Built for a maker with retinitis pigmentosa, so the constraint is lived, not theoretical.

    • The whole hub is a single self-contained file with no runtime dependencies. Data is built to export cleanly. The water metaphor — not personal iconography — is what travels, so another campus can adopt it without inheriting one author's furniture.

      Grounding: built-to-survive design — favor portability over cleverness; a system should outlast software, vendor, subscription, and staffing changes. A metaphor licenses; a mascot does not.

    Built to Last — Succession & Portability

    The short version. Confluence keeps working no matter who is available. It is one self-contained file — no server, no database, no subscription, nothing to keep running. Any institution holding a copy can use it indefinitely with no action required. The tool does not depend on its builder staying available; it was designed that way on purpose.

    The architecture is the succession plan. Because Confluence is a single HTML file that opens in any browser, a successor does not inherit a system to operate — they inherit a document to open. Anyone who can open a web page can run it. Anyone comfortable editing HTML can maintain it. This is the deliberate consequence of the locked principle built to last: favor portability over cleverness, so the tool outlasts software, vendor, subscription, and staffing changes.

    What a successor inherits, and what maintenance takes

    Inherited assets: the canonical file (confluence-TRUNK-v{NN}-YYYY-MM-DD.html, version first, the word “trunk” canonical); the dated version history in the Build Versions folder; The Source, the maintained record of design rationale, locked decisions, and honest proven-vs-needs-validation status; and the four design principles plus the decision tree of settled calls, so the tool can be extended without re-litigating closed questions.

    Maintenance skills, in order of how often they are needed: open and edit a single HTML file in a text editor (most updates); follow the no-branching rule — edit the trunk in place, a feature goes in or it waits; run the build-integrity round-trip on every save (save to Build Versions, confirm the file size is plausible — a save far under the normal size is a likely corruption); and, for new interactive features, working knowledge of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Content updates need none of the last.

    The remaining single points of failure — named honestly

    Portability is strong; it is not absolute. Stated plainly so they can be managed:

    Builder knowledge. Concentrated in one person; mitigated by The Source, which externalizes the rationale and history. Strengthened by keeping it current on every ship — an un-updated copy is a stale copy.

    Storage account. The version history and project files live in one Drive account; mitigated by periodically exporting the canonical file and The Source to a second location not tied to a single institutional login.

    IP and licensing. Owned by one person; no agreement transfers automatically. The ownership terms and the required attorney-review step are documented, so the legal path is defined rather than improvised.

    Convenience automations. Two background watchers flag new and possibly-corrupted saves. They are convenience, not load-bearing — the tool runs without them, and a successor can ignore or rebuild them.

    Still open: the portability commitments not yet finished

    Universal data export — the long-term commitment that any data Confluence holds can leave it cleanly. Until complete, the tool’s portability is structural (the file moves) but not yet fully data-level.

    In sum: Confluence’s sustainability rests on a deliberate choice to make the tool a single portable document with no infrastructure, governed by a written record of why it is built the way it is. The builder is the current steward, not a required runtime. That was designed in, not added on.

    Panel Review Cabinet

    The governance panel's notes on the Watershed Year landscape, kept with the file as the design record — why the feature is built the way it is, and what still stands parked. Each row opens on click or Enter.

    A&OAssessment & Outcomes Specialistreading load▼

    The metaphor is sound and traces to real outcomes. But the reading load is high: five faint tributaries plus five bulges plus four eddies plus six markers plus two label columns. The eye doesn't know where to land. The house principle — low reading load, image over text — is being undercut by density, not by lack of imagery.

    A11YAccessibility Officerconcern▼

    Defect 1: The tributaries at thin stroke width and reduced opacity read as faded — for RP vision that is the first thing to vanish. The constant Written-Communication channel reads; the tributaries don't.

    Defect 2: A forced minimum width pushes the landscape into horizontal scroll on a phone, so the whole picture can't be seen at once at zoom. That's a discoverability failure, not a cosmetic one.

    BIBuild Integrity Wardengate▼

    The Overview architecture SVG carries one inherited unclosed tag. It renders, but the markup is invalid. No SVG surgery on the landscape happens in the same session — that fix lands first, on its own, before any pop work touches the water.

    FACSkeptical Faculty Adopter8-second read▼

    A chair glancing at this for eight seconds sees squiggles. The three things that matter — this year's focus, the constant EN102 spine, the honestly-drawn barriers — aren't visually dominant. Pop means hierarchy, not more ink: one tributary sings, four recede.

    ARCThe Archivistsequence▼

    The dependency chain holds and nothing reorders it: SVG fix → real SLO rotation order → year-tap pop. The emphasis-hierarchy work is the visual prerequisite for the year-tap build — you can't wire “tap a year, light that line” until the lit and dim states exist as design. Real SLO data must precede the tap wiring, or the Assessment gate on fabricated data trips.
